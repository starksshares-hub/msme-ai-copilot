# Financial Ratios for Small Businesses

## Purpose of this document

This document explains important financial ratios used to understand the health of a small business, MSME, shop, trader, manufacturer, or service business.

Financial ratios help answer questions like:

* Is the business profitable?
* Is the business able to pay short-term bills?
* Is the business using too much debt?
* Is inventory moving fast or slow?
* Are customers paying on time?
* Is the business improving or weakening?
* Is cash flow under pressure?
* Is the business financially stable?

Financial ratios should not be read alone. They should be compared with past performance, industry standards, business model, and cash flow situation.

---

# 1. Profitability Ratios

Profitability ratios show whether the business is earning enough profit from sales, assets, and capital.

---

## Gross Profit Margin

### Formula

```text id="dkhsec"
Gross Profit Margin = (Gross Profit / Revenue) × 100
```

Where:

```text
Gross Profit = Revenue - Cost of Goods Sold
```

### Meaning

Gross profit margin shows how much profit is left after deducting direct costs of goods or services.

Direct costs may include:

* Raw materials
* Purchase cost of goods
* Direct labour
* Production cost
* Freight inward
* Factory-related direct costs

### What it signifies

A high gross margin means the business earns good profit before paying operating expenses.

A low gross margin means the business may have high purchase cost, weak pricing, wastage, discounting, or inefficient production.

### How it helps

Gross margin helps in:

* Checking pricing strength
* Understanding product profitability
* Detecting high purchase cost
* Identifying wastage
* Comparing products
* Deciding whether selling price should increase
* Checking if discounts are hurting profit

### Warning signs

* Gross margin falling every month
* Sales increasing but gross profit not increasing
* Cost of goods rising faster than sales
* Heavy discounting
* Inventory wastage
* Poor supplier pricing

---

## Net Profit Margin

### Formula

```text id="93y8dp"
Net Profit Margin = (Net Profit / Revenue) × 100
```

### Meaning

Net profit margin shows how much final profit remains after all expenses, interest, tax, salaries, rent, electricity, and other costs.

### What it signifies

A high net margin means the business controls costs well and keeps good final profit.

A low net margin means the business may be earning sales but not converting them into real profit.

### How it helps

Net margin helps in:

* Measuring final profitability
* Checking cost control
* Comparing business performance over time
* Understanding whether sales growth is actually useful
* Checking whether expenses are too high
* Evaluating business sustainability

### Warning signs

* Revenue increasing but net profit decreasing
* High rent or salary cost
* High interest cost
* Too many unnecessary expenses
* Low pricing power
* Frequent losses

---

## Operating Profit Margin

### Formula

```text id="vrwfmn"
Operating Profit Margin = (Operating Profit / Revenue) × 100
```

### Meaning

Operating profit margin shows profit from main business operations before interest and tax.

Operating profit is also called EBIT in many reports.

```text
Operating Profit = Revenue - Operating Expenses
```

### What it signifies

This ratio shows whether the core business is profitable before financing and tax effects.

### How it helps

Operating margin helps in:

* Checking core business strength
* Ignoring tax and loan effects temporarily
* Comparing operational efficiency
* Understanding whether daily business activities are profitable
* Finding if admin, salary, rent, and marketing costs are too high

### Warning signs

* Operating margin falling
* Operating expenses rising faster than sales
* Business depends only on non-operating income
* Core operations are weak

---

## EBITDA Margin

### Formula

```text id="hpljxx"
EBITDA Margin = (EBITDA / Revenue) × 100
```

Where:

```text
EBITDA = Earnings Before Interest, Tax, Depreciation, and Amortization
```

### Meaning

EBITDA margin shows operating earning power before interest, tax, depreciation, and amortization.

### What it signifies

It shows how much cash-like operating profit the business generates before financing and accounting deductions.

### How it helps

EBITDA margin helps in:

* Checking operating performance
* Comparing businesses with different loan structures
* Understanding business cash generation ability
* Evaluating whether the business can handle debt

### Warning signs

* EBITDA positive but net profit negative due to high interest
* EBITDA falling over time
* High depreciation due to heavy machinery
* Business looks profitable before debt but weak after debt

---

## Return on Assets

### Formula

```text id="f9ovyf"
Return on Assets = (Net Profit / Total Assets) × 100
```

### Meaning

Return on assets shows how efficiently the business uses its assets to generate profit.

Assets include:

* Cash
* Inventory
* Machinery
* Vehicles
* Furniture
* Receivables
* Buildings
* Equipment

### What it signifies

A high ROA means assets are being used efficiently.

A low ROA may mean assets are idle, inventory is stuck, machinery is underused, or profits are too low.

### How it helps

ROA helps in:

* Measuring asset efficiency
* Checking whether investment in assets is useful
* Identifying idle assets
* Comparing profit against total resources used
* Evaluating expansion decisions

### Warning signs

* Many assets but low profit
* Expensive machinery not increasing output
* Inventory lying unsold
* Vehicles or equipment underused

---

## Return on Equity

### Formula

```text id="qn265x"
Return on Equity = (Net Profit / Owner's Equity) × 100
```

### Meaning

Return on equity shows how much profit is earned on the owner’s invested capital.

### What it signifies

A high ROE means the business gives good return on owner investment.

A low ROE means the owner’s capital may not be producing enough profit.

### How it helps

ROE helps in:

* Measuring owner return
* Comparing business return with other investment options
* Checking whether the business is worth expanding
* Understanding long-term wealth creation

### Warning signs

* Owner invests more money but profit does not increase
* Profit is too low compared to capital invested
* High ROE caused only by too much debt can be risky

# 2. Liquidity Ratios

Liquidity ratios show whether the business can pay short-term bills and obligations.

---

## Current Ratio

### Formula

```text id="oee68p"
Current Ratio = Current Assets / Current Liabilities
```

### Meaning

Current ratio shows whether current assets are enough to pay current liabilities.

Current assets include:

* Cash
* Bank balance
* Inventory
* Receivables
* Short-term deposits

Current liabilities include:

* Supplier dues
* Short-term loans
* Outstanding expenses
* GST payable
* Salaries payable
* Current portion of long-term loan

### What it signifies

A current ratio above 1 means current assets are more than current liabilities.

A very low current ratio means the business may struggle to pay bills.

A very high current ratio may mean cash, receivables, or inventory are not being used efficiently.

### How it helps

Current ratio helps in:

* Checking short-term financial health
* Understanding ability to pay suppliers
* Evaluating working capital
* Detecting cash pressure
* Loan eligibility analysis

### Warning signs

* Current ratio below 1
* Supplier payments delayed
* High current liabilities
* Too much inventory but low cash
* Receivables not collected on time

---

## Quick Ratio

### Formula

```text id="rslitq"
Quick Ratio = (Current Assets - Inventory) / Current Liabilities
```

### Meaning

Quick ratio checks whether the business can pay short-term liabilities without depending on selling inventory.

### What it signifies

It is stricter than current ratio because inventory may take time to sell.

A low quick ratio means the business depends heavily on inventory sales to pay bills.

### How it helps

Quick ratio helps in:

* Checking emergency payment ability
* Measuring liquid financial strength
* Identifying overdependence on inventory
* Understanding whether cash and receivables are enough

### Warning signs

* Good current ratio but poor quick ratio
* Too much slow-moving inventory
* Low bank balance
* Receivables not collected
* Business cannot pay bills unless stock sells

---

## Cash Ratio

### Formula

```text id="vv3fba"
Cash Ratio = Cash and Cash Equivalents / Current Liabilities
```

### Meaning

Cash ratio shows how much of the short-term liabilities can be paid immediately using cash or bank balance.

### What it signifies

A high cash ratio means strong immediate liquidity.

A very low cash ratio means the business may face payment pressure even if it has inventory or receivables.

### How it helps

Cash ratio helps in:

* Emergency cash planning
* Checking ability to handle sudden expenses
* Understanding immediate payment strength
* Avoiding cheque bounce or delayed payments

### Warning signs

* Very low bank balance
* Frequent overdraft usage
* Salary or supplier payments delayed
* Cash ratio falling while liabilities rise

# 3. Solvency and Debt Ratios

Solvency ratios show whether the business has too much debt and whether it can survive long term.

---

## Debt Ratio

### Formula

```text id="yrmbdn"
Debt Ratio = Total Liabilities / Total Assets
```

### Meaning

Debt ratio shows how much of the business assets are financed by liabilities.

### What it signifies

A high debt ratio means the business depends heavily on borrowed money or unpaid obligations.

A lower debt ratio usually means lower financial risk.

### How it helps

Debt ratio helps in:

* Checking long-term risk
* Understanding financial stability
* Evaluating lender risk
* Measuring dependence on borrowed funds

### Warning signs

* Debt ratio increasing every year
* Business assets mostly funded by loans
* Profit not enough to repay debt
* New loans taken to repay old loans

---

## Debt-to-Equity Ratio

### Formula

```text id="c5s5wj"
Debt-to-Equity Ratio = Total Debt / Owner's Equity
```

### Meaning

Debt-to-equity ratio compares borrowed funds with owner’s funds.

### What it signifies

A high debt-to-equity ratio means the business uses more debt than owner capital.

This can increase returns when business is profitable, but it also increases risk.

### How it helps

Debt-to-equity ratio helps in:

* Checking financial leverage
* Understanding debt pressure
* Evaluating loan risk
* Comparing owner investment with borrowed money

### Warning signs

* Debt much higher than equity
* Interest cost rising
* Business depends too much on external loans
* Owner has very little capital at risk

---

## Interest Coverage Ratio

### Formula

```text id="a8v32z"
Interest Coverage Ratio = EBIT / Interest Expense
```

### Meaning

Interest coverage ratio shows whether operating profit is enough to pay interest on loans.

### What it signifies

A high ratio means the business can comfortably pay interest.

A low ratio means the business may struggle to service debt.

### How it helps

Interest coverage helps in:

* Loan eligibility analysis
* Checking debt repayment comfort
* Measuring financial risk
* Understanding whether interest cost is too high

### Warning signs

* Ratio below 1
* EBIT barely covers interest
* Interest cost rising faster than profit
* Business needs new loans to pay old interest

---

## Debt Service Coverage Ratio

### Formula

```text id="zl9j0v"
DSCR = Net Operating Income / Total Debt Service
```

Where:

```text
Total Debt Service = Principal Repayment + Interest Payment
```

### Meaning

DSCR shows whether the business earns enough operating income to pay loan principal and interest.

### What it signifies

A DSCR above 1 means income is more than debt repayment.

A DSCR below 1 means income is not enough to fully cover debt payments.

### How it helps

DSCR helps in:

* Bank loan evaluation
* Checking EMI affordability
* Measuring repayment capacity
* Understanding loan stress

### Warning signs

* DSCR below 1
* EMI too high compared to business income
* Business misses EMI during slow months
* Cash flow weak despite profit on paper

# 4. Efficiency and Activity Ratios

Efficiency ratios show how well the business uses inventory, assets, receivables, and payables.

---

## Inventory Turnover Ratio

### Formula

```text id="dim17w"
Inventory Turnover Ratio = Cost of Goods Sold / Average Inventory
```

Where:

```text
Average Inventory = (Opening Inventory + Closing Inventory) / 2
```

### Meaning

Inventory turnover shows how many times inventory is sold and replaced during a period.

### What it signifies

A high inventory turnover means stock is moving quickly.

A low inventory turnover means stock is moving slowly or may be stuck.

### How it helps

Inventory turnover helps in:

* Detecting dead stock
* Planning purchases
* Improving cash flow
* Avoiding overstocking
* Understanding product demand
* Reducing storage cost

### Warning signs

* Low inventory turnover
* Old stock not selling
* High inventory but low cash
* Products becoming outdated
* Heavy discounting needed to sell stock

---

## Inventory Days

### Formula

```text id="bi1ioc"
Inventory Days = 365 / Inventory Turnover Ratio
```

### Meaning

Inventory days show how many days inventory stays in the business before being sold.

### What it signifies

Lower inventory days usually means faster stock movement.

Higher inventory days means stock is sitting longer.

### How it helps

Inventory days helps in:

* Stock planning
* Purchase control
* Cash flow management
* Identifying slow-moving products
* Reducing storage and damage risk

### Warning signs

* Inventory days increasing
* Stock older than normal selling cycle
* Cash blocked in unsold goods
* High wastage or expiry risk

---

## Receivables Turnover Ratio

### Formula

```text id="nejtwd"
Receivables Turnover Ratio = Credit Sales / Average Accounts Receivable
```

### Meaning

Receivables turnover shows how quickly the business collects money from customers.

### What it signifies

A high ratio means customers are paying quickly.

A low ratio means collections are slow.

### How it helps

Receivables turnover helps in:

* Checking collection efficiency
* Managing customer credit
* Detecting overdue invoices
* Improving cash flow
* Reducing bad debts

### Warning signs

* Customers delay payment
* Large unpaid invoices
* Sales made on credit but cash not received
* Business needs loans because receivables are stuck

---

## Receivable Days

### Formula

```text id="q95ptk"
Receivable Days = 365 / Receivables Turnover Ratio
```

Alternative formula:

```text
Receivable Days = (Average Receivables / Credit Sales) × 365
```

### Meaning

Receivable days show the average number of days customers take to pay.

### What it signifies

Lower receivable days means faster collection.

Higher receivable days means money is stuck with customers for longer.

### How it helps

Receivable days helps in:

* Setting payment terms
* Following up with customers
* Detecting risky customers
* Improving working capital
* Planning cash flow

### Warning signs

* Receivable days higher than payment terms
* Customers regularly delay
* Old invoices unpaid
* Cash shortage despite strong sales

---

## Payables Turnover Ratio

### Formula

```text id="6i9cxi"
Payables Turnover Ratio = Credit Purchases / Average Accounts Payable
```

### Meaning

Payables turnover shows how quickly the business pays suppliers.

### What it signifies

A high ratio means suppliers are paid quickly.

A low ratio means supplier payments are slower.

### How it helps

Payables turnover helps in:

* Understanding supplier payment discipline
* Managing working capital
* Avoiding supplier disputes
* Planning cash outflows
* Negotiating credit terms

### Warning signs

* Supplier payments delayed too long
* Supplier stops giving credit
* Business depends on unpaid suppliers
* Late fees or damaged relationships

---

## Payable Days

### Formula

```text id="w82h5h"
Payable Days = 365 / Payables Turnover Ratio
```

Alternative formula:

```text
Payable Days = (Average Payables / Credit Purchases) × 365
```

### Meaning

Payable days show how many days the business takes to pay suppliers.

### What it signifies

Higher payable days means the business takes longer to pay suppliers.

This can help cash flow, but if too high, it may damage supplier trust.

### How it helps

Payable days helps in:

* Supplier payment planning
* Cash flow management
* Negotiating better credit periods
* Avoiding overdue supplier dues

### Warning signs

* Payable days rising because of cash shortage
* Supplier complaints
* Loss of supplier credit
* Late payment penalties

---

## Asset Turnover Ratio

### Formula

```text id="xv8rbp"
Asset Turnover Ratio = Revenue / Average Total Assets
```

### Meaning

Asset turnover shows how efficiently assets generate sales.

### What it signifies

A high ratio means assets are being used effectively to create revenue.

A low ratio means assets may be idle or underused.

### How it helps

Asset turnover helps in:

* Measuring asset efficiency
* Checking whether machinery or equipment is productive
* Comparing sales with investment in assets
* Finding underused assets

### Warning signs

* Expensive assets but low sales
* Idle machinery
* Excess capacity
* Poor utilization of shop, warehouse, or factory

---

## Working Capital Turnover Ratio

### Formula

```text id="l1ofxk"
Working Capital Turnover = Revenue / Average Working Capital
```

Where:

```text
Working Capital = Current Assets - Current Liabilities
```

### Meaning

Working capital turnover shows how efficiently working capital is used to generate sales.

### What it signifies

A high ratio means the business generates strong sales from available working capital.

A very high ratio may also mean working capital is too low and the business may face liquidity pressure.

### How it helps

Working capital turnover helps in:

* Measuring working capital efficiency
* Checking whether money is stuck in inventory or receivables
* Understanding cash flow management
* Evaluating business growth capacity

### Warning signs

* Working capital too low
* Sales high but cash pressure high
* Receivables and inventory poorly managed
* Supplier dues increasing

---

# 5. Cash Flow Ratios

Cash flow ratios show whether profit is converting into real cash.

---

## Operating Cash Flow Ratio

### Formula

```text id="neoe2a"
Operating Cash Flow Ratio = Operating Cash Flow / Current Liabilities
```

### Meaning

This ratio shows whether cash from operations is enough to pay short-term liabilities.

### What it signifies

A high ratio means strong cash generation.

A low ratio means the business may be profitable on paper but weak in cash.

### How it helps

Operating cash flow ratio helps in:

* Checking real cash strength
* Understanding ability to pay short-term dues
* Detecting profit-cash mismatch
* Evaluating business sustainability

### Warning signs

* Profit positive but operating cash flow negative
* Receivables increasing
* Inventory increasing too much
* Supplier dues unpaid
* Cash flow weaker than accounting profit

---

## Cash Conversion Cycle

### Formula

```text id="fiyqh0"
Cash Conversion Cycle = Inventory Days + Receivable Days - Payable Days
```

### Meaning

Cash conversion cycle shows how many days cash is stuck in the business cycle.

It measures the time between paying for inventory and collecting cash from customers.

### What it signifies

A shorter cash conversion cycle is usually better.

A longer cycle means cash is blocked for more days.

### How it helps

Cash conversion cycle helps in:

* Understanding working capital pressure
* Managing inventory and receivables
* Planning loans or cash credit
* Improving business cash flow

### Warning signs

* Inventory days increasing
* Receivable days increasing
* Payable days decreasing too much
* Business needs frequent working capital loans
* Cash stuck despite good sales

---

# 6. Expense Ratios

Expense ratios show which expenses are consuming revenue.

---

## Operating Expense Ratio

### Formula

```text id="cwutl5"
Operating Expense Ratio = (Operating Expenses / Revenue) × 100
```

### Meaning

Operating expense ratio shows how much of revenue is used for day-to-day operating expenses.

Operating expenses may include:

* Rent
* Salaries
* Electricity
* Marketing
* Repairs
* Transport
* Office expenses
* Software expenses

### What it signifies

A high operating expense ratio means expenses are taking a large portion of sales.

A low ratio means the business controls expenses well.

### How it helps

Operating expense ratio helps in:

* Cost control
* Budget planning
* Identifying unnecessary expenses
* Improving profitability
* Comparing expenses month by month

### Warning signs

* Expenses rising faster than sales
* Rent too high
* Salary cost too high
* Marketing spend not creating sales
* Uncontrolled admin costs

---

## Salary Expense Ratio

### Formula

```text id="87skhm"
Salary Expense Ratio = (Salary Expense / Revenue) × 100
```

### Meaning

Salary expense ratio shows how much of revenue is spent on employee wages and salaries.

### What it signifies

A high ratio may mean labour cost is too high compared to sales.

A low ratio may mean the business is lean, but it could also mean understaffing.

### How it helps

Salary expense ratio helps in:

* Hiring decisions
* Productivity analysis
* Cost control
* Department-wise performance review

### Warning signs

* Salary cost rising but sales flat
* Too many employees for current sales
* Low productivity per employee
* Overtime cost increasing

---

## Rent Expense Ratio

### Formula

```text id="u1dcxg"
Rent Expense Ratio = (Rent Expense / Revenue) × 100
```

### Meaning

Rent expense ratio shows how much sales revenue is consumed by rent.

### What it signifies

A high rent ratio means location cost may be too heavy for the business.

### How it helps

Rent ratio helps in:

* Evaluating shop or office location
* Deciding whether to expand
* Negotiating rent
* Checking branch profitability

### Warning signs

* Rent high but footfall low
* Sales not enough to justify location
* Rent increasing faster than revenue
* New branch becomes loss-making due to rent

---

## Interest Expense Ratio

### Formula

```text id="tasvrj"
Interest Expense Ratio = (Interest Expense / Revenue) × 100
```

### Meaning

Interest expense ratio shows how much of revenue is used to pay loan interest.

### What it signifies

A high interest ratio means debt cost is eating into business income.

### How it helps

Interest expense ratio helps in:

* Checking debt pressure
* Deciding whether to take new loans
* Evaluating refinancing options
* Understanding financial risk

### Warning signs

* Interest cost rising every month
* Revenue not increasing after taking loans
* New loans needed to pay old loans
* Net profit falling because of interest

---

# 7. Growth Ratios

Growth ratios show whether the business is improving over time.

---

## Revenue Growth Rate

### Formula

```text id="1pn96w"
Revenue Growth Rate = [(Current Period Revenue - Previous Period Revenue) / Previous Period Revenue] × 100
```

### Meaning

Revenue growth rate shows how much sales increased or decreased compared to a previous period.

### What it signifies

Positive revenue growth means sales are increasing.

Negative revenue growth means sales are declining.

### How it helps

Revenue growth helps in:

* Tracking business expansion
* Identifying seasonal patterns
* Checking marketing impact
* Planning inventory
* Forecasting cash flow

### Warning signs

* Revenue falling continuously
* Growth only due to credit sales not collected
* Sales growth but profit falling
* Growth caused only by heavy discounts

---

## Profit Growth Rate

### Formula

```text id="6n8caa"
Profit Growth Rate = [(Current Period Profit - Previous Period Profit) / Previous Period Profit] × 100
```

### Meaning

Profit growth rate shows whether profit is increasing or decreasing.

### What it signifies

Profit growth is stronger than sales growth because it shows actual improvement in earning.

### How it helps

Profit growth helps in:

* Measuring real business improvement
* Checking cost control
* Evaluating expansion success
* Understanding whether revenue growth is meaningful

### Warning signs

* Sales increasing but profit decreasing
* Expenses rising too fast
* Gross margin falling
* Interest cost reducing final profit

---

# 8. Customer and Credit Ratios

These ratios are useful when the business sells goods or services on credit.

---

## Bad Debt Ratio

### Formula

```text id="obbi1b"
Bad Debt Ratio = (Bad Debts / Credit Sales) × 100
```

### Meaning

Bad debt ratio shows how much credit sales could not be collected from customers.

### What it signifies

A high bad debt ratio means poor credit control or risky customers.

### How it helps

Bad debt ratio helps in:

* Improving customer credit policy
* Reducing risky sales
* Identifying bad customers
* Protecting cash flow

### Warning signs

* Bad debts increasing
* Same customers delaying repeatedly
* Sales team gives credit without checking customer reliability
* No follow-up system for collections

---

## Credit Sales Ratio

### Formula

```text id="b251s4"
Credit Sales Ratio = (Credit Sales / Total Sales) × 100
```

### Meaning

Credit sales ratio shows how much of total sales are made on credit.

### What it signifies

A high ratio means the business depends heavily on customer payments in the future.

### How it helps

Credit sales ratio helps in:

* Managing collection risk
* Planning working capital
* Setting customer payment terms
* Avoiding cash shortages

### Warning signs

* Credit sales too high
* Cash sales low
* Customers delay payment
* Business needs loans because cash is not coming in

| Operating Expense Ratio   | Operating Expenses / Revenue × 100                   | Expense burden                | Higher cost burden                            | Better cost control         |
| Salary Expense Ratio      | Salary / Revenue × 100                               | Staff cost burden             | Higher employee cost                          | Lower employee cost         |
| Rent Expense Ratio        | Rent / Revenue × 100                                 | Location cost burden          | Rent pressure                                 | Lower rent pressure         |
| Interest Expense Ratio    | Interest / Revenue × 100                             | Debt cost burden              | High debt cost                                | Lower debt cost             |
| Revenue Growth Rate       | Change in Revenue / Previous Revenue × 100           | Sales growth                  | Sales increasing                              | Sales declining             |
| Profit Growth Rate        | Change in Profit / Previous Profit × 100             | Profit growth                 | Profit improving                              | Profit weakening            |
| Bad Debt Ratio            | Bad Debts / Credit Sales × 100                       | Uncollected credit sales      | High customer risk                            | Better collection           |
| Credit Sales Ratio        | Credit Sales / Total Sales × 100                     | Dependence on credit sales    | More collection risk                          | More cash sales             |

# 11. Limitations of Financial Ratios

Financial ratios are useful, but they have limitations.

Ratios may be misleading if:

* Accounting records are wrong
* Sales are fake or inflated
* Inventory value is incorrect
* Receivables include very old unpaid invoices
* Expenses are hidden
* Personal and business expenses are mixed
* One-time income or expense is included
* Business is seasonal
* Industry comparison is missing
* Cash flow is ignored
* Tax records are incomplete

A ratio should always be checked with actual invoices, bank statements, cash flow, stock records, and business context.

# 12. Common user questions this document can answer

## What is gross profit margin?

Gross profit margin shows how much profit remains after direct product or service costs are deducted from revenue.

## What does net profit margin mean?

Net profit margin shows how much final profit the business keeps from sales after all expenses.

## What does current ratio show?

Current ratio shows whether current assets are enough to pay current liabilities.

## What does debt ratio mean?

Debt ratio shows how much of the business is funded by liabilities.

## What does inventory turnover mean?

Inventory turnover shows how quickly stock is sold and replaced.

## Why can a business have good sales but poor cash flow?

This can happen when customers delay payments, inventory is overstocked, expenses are high, or EMI burden is too heavy.

## Which ratios help check loan repayment ability?

Important ratios for loan repayment include DSCR, interest coverage ratio, debt-to-equity ratio, current ratio, and operating cash flow ratio.

## Which ratios help check inventory problems?

Inventory turnover, inventory days, quick ratio, and cash conversion cycle help identify inventory problems.

## Which ratios help check customer payment problems?

Receivables turnover, receivable days, bad debt ratio, and credit sales ratio help check customer payment problems.
