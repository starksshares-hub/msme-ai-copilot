# Accounts Receivable for MSMEs

Accounts receivable means money that customers owe to a business for goods or services already sold on credit.

For an MSME, accounts receivable usually comes from unpaid customer invoices. The business has already made the sale, delivered goods, or completed the service, but the customer has not paid yet.

Accounts receivable is an asset because the business expects to receive cash in the future.

---

## Simple Meaning

If a business sells goods worth ₹50,000 to a customer today and gives 30 days to pay, then ₹50,000 becomes accounts receivable until the customer pays.

```text
Sale made on credit = Accounts receivable created
Customer pays later = Accounts receivable reduced
```

---

## Why Accounts Receivable Matters

Accounts receivable is important because sales are not useful if cash is not collected on time.

A business may show profit but still face cash shortage if customers delay payment.

For MSMEs, delayed customer payments can create serious problems such as:

```text
- Not enough cash to pay suppliers
- Difficulty paying salaries
- Late GST or tax payments
- Loan repayment pressure
- Need for extra working capital loan
- Poor cash flow despite good sales
```

---

# Key Terms

## 1. Credit Sale

A credit sale happens when the business sells goods or services but allows the customer to pay later.

Example:

```text
Invoice amount: ₹80,000
Payment terms: 30 days
Customer pays later

This is a credit sale.
```

---

## 2. Accounts Receivable

Accounts receivable is the total amount customers owe to the business.

Example:

```text
Customer A owes ₹20,000
Customer B owes ₹35,000
Customer C owes ₹15,000

Total accounts receivable = ₹70,000
```

---

## 3. Trade Debtors

Trade debtors is another common accounting term for accounts receivable.

In simple words:

```text
Accounts receivable = Trade debtors = Money to be collected from customers
```

---

## 4. Invoice Due Date

The due date is the date by which the customer should pay the invoice.

Example:

```text
Invoice date: 1 April 2025
Payment terms: 30 days
Due date: 1 May 2025
```

If the customer does not pay by 1 May 2025, the invoice becomes overdue.

---

## 5. Overdue Invoice

An overdue invoice is an invoice that has not been paid after its due date.

Example:

```text
Invoice due date: 10 May 2025
Today: 20 May 2025

Invoice is overdue by 10 days.
```

---

## 6. Aging

Aging means grouping unpaid invoices based on how old they are.

Aging helps the business understand which invoices are fresh, which are overdue, and which may be difficult to collect.

Common aging buckets:

```text
0-30 days
31-60 days
61-90 days
More than 90 days
```

---

## 7. Collection Risk

Collection risk means the risk that a customer may delay payment or never pay.

The older an invoice becomes, the higher the collection risk usually becomes.

# How to Calculate Days Overdue

```text
Days Overdue = Today’s Date - Invoice Due Date
```

Example:

```text
Today’s date = 10 June 2025
Invoice due date = 30 April 2025

Days overdue = 41 days
```

If today’s date is before the due date, the invoice is not overdue.

---

# How to Calculate Outstanding Amount

```text
Outstanding Amount = Invoice Amount - Paid Amount
```

Example:

```text
Invoice amount = ₹35,000
Paid amount = ₹10,000

Outstanding amount = ₹25,000
```

---

# Accounts Receivable in Balance Sheet

Accounts receivable appears under current assets in the balance sheet because the business expects to collect the money within one year.

Example:

```text
Current Assets:
Cash in Hand: ₹50,000
Bank Balance: ₹2,00,000
Accounts Receivable: ₹1,80,000
Inventory: ₹3,00,000
```

Accounts receivable increases current assets, but it is not the same as cash.

---

# Accounts Receivable and Cash Flow

Accounts receivable is one of the biggest reasons MSMEs face cash flow problems.

A business may have high sales but low cash if customers have not paid.

Example:

```text
Monthly sales: ₹8,00,000
Accounts receivable: ₹5,50,000
Cash in bank: ₹70,000
Supplier payments due: ₹2,00,000
Salary due: ₹1,00,000
```

This business may look successful because sales are high, but it may still struggle because cash has not been collected.

# Good Accounts Receivable Practices

## 1. Set Clear Payment Terms

Every invoice should mention payment terms clearly.

Examples:

```text
Payment due within 15 days
Payment due within 30 days
50% advance, 50% after delivery
Payment due on receipt
```

---

## 2. Send Invoices Quickly

Delayed invoicing causes delayed payment.

If goods are delivered today but the invoice is sent after 10 days, the customer may pay even later.

---

## 3. Track Due Dates

Every invoice should have a due date.

The business should know:

```text
- Which invoices are due today
- Which invoices are overdue
- Which customers delay payment often
- How much total money is pending
```

---

## 4. Follow Up Before Due Date

MSMEs should not wait until invoices become very old.

A good follow-up system:

```text
5 days before due date: polite reminder
On due date: payment reminder
7 days after due date: stronger follow-up
30 days overdue: escalation
60+ days overdue: management/legal review
```

---

## 5. Give Credit Carefully

Not every customer should receive long credit periods.

Before giving credit, check:

```text
- Customer payment history
- Order size
- Relationship with customer
- Business reputation
- Past overdue invoices
- Credit limit
```

---

## 6. Use Credit Limits

A credit limit is the maximum unpaid amount allowed for a customer.

Example:

```text
Customer credit limit: ₹1,00,000
Current outstanding: ₹90,000
New order: ₹40,000

Total exposure after new order = ₹1,30,000
```

The new order should not be approved without payment or special approval.

---

## 7. Match Payments to Invoices

When payment is received, it should be matched to the correct invoice.

This avoids confusion such as:

```text
- Customer claims they paid
- Business cannot identify which invoice was paid
- Same payment counted twice
- Old invoice still shown as unpaid
```

# Red Flags in Accounts Receivable

## 1. Too Many Overdue Invoices

If many invoices are overdue, the business may face cash flow pressure.

Red flag:

```text
More than 30% of receivables are overdue beyond 60 days.
```

---

## 2. High Receivables Compared to Sales

If accounts receivable is very high compared to monthly sales, customers may be taking too long to pay.

Example:

```text
Monthly sales: ₹5,00,000
Accounts receivable: ₹10,00,000
```

This means two months of sales are stuck in receivables.

---

## 3. One Customer Owes Too Much

If one customer owes a large share of total receivables, the business has customer concentration risk.

Example:

```text
Total receivables: ₹8,00,000
One customer owes: ₹5,00,000
```

If that customer delays payment, the business may suffer badly.

---

## 4. Old Invoices Still Unpaid

Invoices older than 90 days are risky.

They may become doubtful debts or bad debts.

---

## 5. Repeated Partial Payments

Partial payments are not always bad, but repeated small payments may indicate the customer has cash problems.

---

## 6. Customer Disputes

Invoices under dispute may not be collected quickly.

Common dispute reasons:

```text
- Wrong quantity
- Wrong rate
- Damaged goods
- Service not completed
- GST mismatch
- Missing purchase order
- Delivery proof missing
```

# Collection Risk Levels

## Low Risk

```text
Invoice is not due yet
Customer has good payment history
Amount is small
No dispute
Customer usually pays on time
```

## Medium Risk

```text
Invoice is 1-30 days overdue
Customer is slow but responsive
Partial payment received
No major dispute
```

## High Risk

```text
Invoice is 31-90 days overdue
Customer delays frequently
Customer gives excuses
Large amount outstanding
Dispute unresolved
```

## Very High Risk

```text
Invoice is more than 90 days overdue
Customer is not responding
Customer refuses to pay
Business relationship has broken
Legal recovery may be needed
```

# Important Accounts Receivable Metrics

## 1. Total Accounts Receivable

```text
Total Accounts Receivable = Sum of all outstanding customer invoice amounts
```

Example:

```text
₹50,000 + ₹25,000 + ₹60,000 + ₹45,000 = ₹1,80,000
```

---

## 2. Overdue Receivables

```text
Overdue Receivables = Sum of all invoices past due date
```

---

## 3. Receivables Aging Percentage

```text
Aging Percentage = Amount in Aging Bucket / Total Receivables × 100
```

Example:

```text
31-60 days overdue amount = ₹75,000
Total receivables = ₹1,80,000

31-60 days aging percentage = ₹75,000 / ₹1,80,000 × 100
31-60 days aging percentage = 41.67%
```

---

## 4. Average Collection Period

Average collection period shows how many days it takes to collect payment from customers.

```text
Average Collection Period = Accounts Receivable / Average Daily Credit Sales
```

Where:

```text
Average Daily Credit Sales = Annual Credit Sales / 365
```

Example:

```text
Accounts receivable = ₹3,00,000
Annual credit sales = ₹36,50,000

Average daily credit sales = ₹36,50,000 / 365
Average daily credit sales = ₹10,000

Average collection period = ₹3,00,000 / ₹10,000
Average collection period = 30 days
```

---

## 5. Days Sales Outstanding

Days Sales Outstanding, also called DSO, shows how many days of sales are stuck in receivables.

```text
DSO = Accounts Receivable / Total Credit Sales × Number of Days
```

Example:

```text
Accounts receivable = ₹4,00,000
Credit sales for month = ₹8,00,000
Number of days = 30

DSO = ₹4,00,000 / ₹8,00,000 × 30
DSO = 15 days
```

A lower DSO usually means faster collection.

# Journal Entries

## 1. When Credit Sale Happens

```text
Accounts Receivable A/c Dr.
    To Sales A/c
```

Example:

```text
Accounts Receivable A/c Dr. ₹50,000
    To Sales A/c ₹50,000
```

This means the business has made a sale, but cash has not been received yet.

---

## 2. When Payment Is Received

```text
Bank A/c Dr.
    To Accounts Receivable A/c
```

Example:

```text
Bank A/c Dr. ₹50,000
    To Accounts Receivable A/c ₹50,000
```

This means cash/bank balance increases and receivable decreases.

---

## 3. When Partial Payment Is Received

Invoice amount: ₹50,000
Payment received: ₹20,000
Remaining receivable: ₹30,000

```text
Bank A/c Dr. ₹20,000
    To Accounts Receivable A/c ₹20,000
```

---

## 4. When Customer Does Not Pay and Amount Becomes Bad Debt

```text
Bad Debts A/c Dr.
    To Accounts Receivable A/c
```

Example:

```text
Bad Debts A/c Dr. ₹10,000
    To Accounts Receivable A/c ₹10,000
```

This removes the uncollectible amount from receivables and records it as a loss/expense.

# Rules for AI/RAG Assistant

## Rule 1: Identify Outstanding Amount

```text
If invoice_amount > paid_amount:
    invoice has outstanding amount
```

---

## Rule 2: Identify Fully Paid Invoice

```text
If paid_amount = invoice_amount:
    payment_status = "paid"
```

---

## Rule 3: Identify Partly Paid Invoice

```text
If paid_amount > 0 and paid_amount < invoice_amount:
    payment_status = "partly paid"
```

---

## Rule 4: Identify Unpaid Invoice

```text
If paid_amount = 0:
    payment_status = "unpaid"
```

---

## Rule 5: Identify Overdue Invoice

```text
If today > due_date and outstanding_amount > 0:
    payment_status = "overdue"
```

---

## Rule 6: Assign Aging Bucket

```text
If days_overdue <= 0:
    aging_bucket = "not due"

If days_overdue >= 1 and days_overdue <= 30:
    aging_bucket = "0-30 days overdue"

If days_overdue >= 31 and days_overdue <= 60:
    aging_bucket = "31-60 days overdue"

If days_overdue >= 61 and days_overdue <= 90:
    aging_bucket = "61-90 days overdue"

If days_overdue > 90:
    aging_bucket = "more than 90 days overdue"
```

---

## Rule 7: Assign Collection Risk

```text
Not due = Low risk
0-30 days overdue = Low to medium risk
31-60 days overdue = Medium risk
61-90 days overdue = High risk
More than 90 days overdue = Very high risk
```

---

## Rule 8: Flag Customer Concentration Risk

```text
If one customer owes more than 40% of total receivables:
    flag customer concentration risk
```

---

## Rule 9: Flag Credit Limit Breach

```text
If customer_outstanding > customer_credit_limit:
    flag credit limit breach
```

---

## Rule 10: Flag Cash Flow Risk

```text
If overdue_receivables are high and cash balance is low:
    flag cash flow risk
```

# RAG Questions This File Can Answer

## Question: What is accounts receivable?

Answer: Accounts receivable is the money customers owe to a business for goods or services already sold on credit. It is shown as a current asset in the balance sheet.

---

## Question: Why is accounts receivable important for MSMEs?

Answer: Accounts receivable is important because delayed customer payments can create cash flow problems. An MSME may have good sales but still struggle to pay suppliers, salaries, rent, GST, or loans if customers do not pay on time.

---

## Question: What is an overdue invoice?

Answer: An overdue invoice is an unpaid or partly paid invoice that has passed its due date.

---

## Question: How do I calculate outstanding amount?

Answer: Outstanding amount is calculated as invoice amount minus paid amount.

```text
Outstanding Amount = Invoice Amount - Paid Amount
```

---

## Question: What is aging in accounts receivable?

Answer: Aging means grouping unpaid invoices based on how many days they are overdue. Common aging buckets are 0-30 days, 31-60 days, 61-90 days, and more than 90 days.

---

## Question: What does high receivables mean?

Answer: High receivables mean a large amount of money is stuck with customers. This can create cash flow problems, especially if the business needs money to pay suppliers, salaries, rent, or taxes.

---

Answer: Collection risk is the risk that a customer may delay payment or not pay at all. Older invoices usually have higher collection risk.

---

## Question: What should a business do when invoices are overdue?

Answer: The business should send reminders, call the customer, check for disputes, request a payment date, stop further credit if needed, and escalate old unpaid invoices.

---

## Question: Is accounts receivable cash?

Answer: No. Accounts receivable is not cash. It is money expected from customers. Cash is only received when the customer actually pays.

---

## Question: Where does accounts receivable appear in the balance sheet?

Answer: Accounts receivable appears under current assets in the balance sheet.

# Simple Summary

Accounts receivable is money customers owe to the business.

For MSMEs, accounts receivable must be tracked carefully because unpaid invoices can create cash flow problems.

The business should monitor:

```text
- Total unpaid invoices
- Overdue invoices
- Days overdue
- Customer-wise outstanding
- Aging buckets
- Collection risk
- Credit limits
- Disputed invoices
```

A healthy business collects receivables on time, follows up regularly, and avoids giving too much credit to risky customers.
