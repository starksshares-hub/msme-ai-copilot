Regulatory Compliance and Tax Reporting StandardsTo enforce financial discipline and protect small businesses from payment delays, the government has introduced strict legislative and tax-related measures.Section 43B(h) of the Income Tax Act, 1961Introduced via the Finance Act 2023 and applicable from the Assessment Year (AY) 2024-25 (corresponding to the Financial Year 2023-24) onwards, Section 43B(h) is a powerful regulatory tool designed to ensure timely payments to small enterprises. It links a buyer's tax deductions directly to their payment discipline.Exact Legal Requirements and TimelinesThe provision mandates that any sum payable to an enterprise classified as a Micro or Small enterprise for goods supplied or services rendered must be paid within the strict timelines stipulated under Section 15 of the MSMED Act, 2006.These timelines are determined by the presence or absence of a written agreement:In the Absence of a Written Agreement: Payment must be made within 15 days from the actual "day of acceptance" of the goods or services.Under a Written Agreement: Payment must be made in accordance with the agreed terms, subject to a statutory ceiling of 45 days from the "day of acceptance".The "day of acceptance" refers to the day of physical delivery of goods or completion of services. However, if the buyer raises a written objection regarding the quality or specifications of the delivery within 15 days, the payment clock is paused. It resumes only when the dispute is resolved by the supplier.Tax Disallowance MechanismsIf a buyer fails to settle their dues to a registered Micro or Small enterprise within the 15 or 45-day window, the outstanding expense is disallowed as a deduction under Section 43B(h) for that financial year. The amount is added back to the buyer's taxable income, increasing their tax liability. The deduction can only be claimed in the financial year in which the actual payment is made, regardless of the mercantile accounting system used by the buyer.Importantly, Section 43B(h) is excluded from the first proviso to Section 43B. For other expenses governed by Section 43B (such as taxes, duties, or employee provident fund contributions), deductions are allowed if the payment is made before the due date for filing the income tax return under Section 139(1).This safety net does not apply to Section 43B(h). Any payment outstanding past the MSMED deadline as of March 31 cannot be claimed in that year, even if it is cleared prior to filing the tax return.Statutory Interest PenaltiesIn addition to tax disallowance, any delay beyond the Section 15 limits triggers a mandatory compound interest liability under Section 16 of the MSMED Act, 2006. The buyer is liable to pay interest compounded monthly at three times the bank rate notified by the RBI. Crucially, under Section 23 of the MSMED Act, this interest is categorized as non-deductible; it cannot be claimed as a business expense to reduce the buyer's taxable income.Critical Exclusions and ExemptionsMedium Enterprises: The provisions of Section 43B(h) apply strictly to Micro and Small enterprises. Medium enterprises are excluded from this mechanism.Mandatory Udyam Registration: The supplier must possess a valid Udyam Registration Certificate at the time of the transaction to trigger Section 43B(h) protections. Unregistered enterprises do not qualify.Traders Excluded: While retail and wholesale traders are permitted to register on the Udyam portal to access Priority Sector Lending (PSL) benefits, they are excluded from the delayed payment provisions of the MSMED Act. Consequently, purchases made from traders are not subject to Section 43B(h) disallowances.Presumptive Taxation Cases: Buyers who calculate their taxable income under the presumptive taxation schemes (such as Section 44AD/44ADA) are exempt from Section 43B(h), as their income is calculated using a flat rate of turnover without detailed deductions.Capital Expenditures: Capital purchases are generally not subject to Section 43B(h) disallowances, unless the capital expenditure is directly eligible for full deduction under specific provisions (such as Section 35AD). Standard depreciation claims on capital assets are unaffected by payment delays.GST Component Treatment: If the buyer claims the GST component of an invoice as an Input Tax Credit (ITC), that portion is treated separately and is not subject to Section 43B(h). However, if the GST is claimed as a regular business expense in the Profit & Loss statement, its deductibility is subject to the timely payment rules.Corporate Compliance: Form MSME-1To ensure transparency, the Ministry of Corporate Affairs (MCA) mandates that any company purchasing goods or services from registered Micro and Small Enterprises and having outstanding dues exceeding 45 days must file Form MSME-1 on a half-yearly basis.This filing requires companies to explicitly disclose:The exact amount of outstanding dues.The names and Udyam numbers of the affected MSE suppliers.The specific reasons for the payment delay.The upward revision of MSME thresholds in 2025 significantly expands the number of eligible Micro and Small enterprises. As millions of businesses transition into these categories, buying companies must implement precise creditor tracking and ledger aging analysis to avoid tax disallowance under Section 43B(h) and ensure compliance with half-yearly Form MSME-1 filing requirements.
