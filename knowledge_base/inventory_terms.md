# Inventory Terms for MSMEs

Inventory means the goods, products, raw materials, or stock that a business keeps for selling, production, or daily operations.

For MSMEs, inventory tracking is important because too much stock blocks cash, while too little stock can stop sales or production.

---

## Why Inventory Tracking Matters for MSMEs

Inventory affects:

* Cash flow
* Sales
* Customer orders
* Production
* Storage cost
* Profit margins
* Business planning

Example:

If a shop buys ₹1,00,000 worth of stock but only sells ₹30,000 quickly, the remaining ₹70,000 is stuck in inventory. This reduces available cash for rent, salaries, supplier payments, or new purchases.

---

## SKU

SKU means **Stock Keeping Unit**.

An SKU is a unique code used to identify a product.

Example:

A mobile accessories shop may use:

```text
CHG-SAM-25W-BLK
```

This could mean:

```text
Samsung 25W Charger Black
```

SKUs help businesses track each product separately.

---

## Stock

Stock means the quantity of goods available in the business.

Example:

```text
Product: LED Bulb
Available stock: 120 units
```

Stock can include:

* Finished goods
* Raw materials
* Work-in-progress goods
* Packaging materials
* Spare parts

---

## Opening Stock

Opening stock means the inventory available at the beginning of an accounting period.

Example:

```text
Opening stock on 1 April: ₹50,000
```

This is usually the closing stock from the previous period.

---

## Closing Stock

Closing stock means the inventory left at the end of an accounting period.

Example:

```text
Closing stock on 31 March: ₹80,000
```

Closing stock is important for calculating profit.

Basic formula:

```text
Cost of Goods Sold = Opening Stock + Purchases - Closing Stock
```

---

## Reorder Level

Reorder level means the stock level at which a business should place a new order.

Example:

```text
Product: Printer Paper
Reorder level: 20 packets
Current stock: 18 packets
Action: Place new order
```

Reorder level helps avoid stockouts.

Simple formula:

```text
Reorder Level = Average Daily Usage × Lead Time
```

Example:

```text
Average daily usage = 10 units
Supplier delivery time = 5 days

Reorder Level = 10 × 5 = 50 units
```

The business should reorder when stock reaches 50 units.

---

## Lead Time

Lead time means the time taken between placing an order and receiving the goods.

Example:

```text
Order placed: 1 July
Goods received: 6 July
Lead time: 5 days
```

Longer lead time means the business must reorder earlier.

---

## Safety Stock

Safety stock is extra stock kept to avoid shortages.

Example:

A shop normally sells 10 units per day. Sometimes demand suddenly increases. To stay safe, the shop keeps 30 extra units.

Safety stock protects the business from:

* Supplier delays
* Sudden demand increase
* Transport issues
* Production delays

---

## Stockout

Stockout means the business has run out of a product.

Example:

```text
Customer wants: 5 kg rice bag
Available stock: 0
Result: Stockout
```

Stockouts can cause:

* Lost sales
* Unhappy customers
* Production delays
* Loss of trust

---

## Overstock

Overstock means the business has more stock than needed.

Example:

```text
Required monthly stock: 100 units
Actual stock: 500 units
```

Overstock can create problems:

* Cash gets blocked
* Storage cost increases
* Goods may expire
* Products may become outdated
* Discount selling may be needed

---

## Dead Stock

Dead stock means inventory that is not selling or has no demand.

Example:

A clothing shop has old fashion stock from last season that customers no longer want.

Dead stock blocks money and storage space.

Common causes of dead stock:

* Wrong demand prediction
* Buying too much
* Product became outdated
* Poor product quality
* Seasonal demand ended
* Wrong pricing

Possible actions:

* Offer discount
* Bundle with fast-moving items
* Return to supplier if possible
* Liquidate stock
* Stop reordering that item

---

## Fast-Moving Inventory

Fast-moving inventory means products that sell quickly.

Example:

For a grocery store:

```text
Milk, bread, rice, atta, oil
```

Fast-moving products usually need frequent reordering.

Benefits:

* Quick cash recovery
* Higher sales volume
* Better stock rotation

Risk:

If not tracked properly, fast-moving items can go out of stock quickly.

---

## Slow-Moving Inventory

Slow-moving inventory means products that sell slowly.

Example:

An electronics shop may sell phone chargers daily, but sell expensive speakers only once or twice a month.

Slow-moving inventory can block cash.

Possible actions:

* Reduce purchase quantity
* Improve product display
* Offer discounts
* Bundle with other products
* Avoid frequent reordering

---

## Inventory Turnover

Inventory turnover shows how quickly stock is sold and replaced.

Formula:

```text
Inventory Turnover = Cost of Goods Sold / Average Inventory
```

Average inventory formula:

```text
Average Inventory = (Opening Stock + Closing Stock) / 2
```

Example:

```text
Cost of Goods Sold = ₹6,00,000
Opening Stock = ₹1,00,000
Closing Stock = ₹2,00,000

Average Inventory = (₹1,00,000 + ₹2,00,000) / 2
Average Inventory = ₹1,50,000

Inventory Turnover = ₹6,00,000 / ₹1,50,000
Inventory Turnover = 4 times
```

This means the business sold and replaced its inventory 4 times during the period.

Higher turnover usually means stock is moving well.

Very low turnover may mean dead stock or slow sales.

---

## Stock Valuation

Stock valuation means deciding the value of inventory in money terms.

Example:

```text
100 units × ₹50 cost price = ₹5,000 stock value
```

Stock valuation affects:

* Profit calculation
* Balance sheet
* Tax reporting
* Business valuation
* Loan applications

---

## Batch Number

A batch number is used to track a group of products produced or purchased together.

This is common in:

* Food products
* Medicines
* Cosmetics
* Chemicals
* Manufacturing

Example:

```text
Batch No: B2026-07-A
```

Batch tracking helps in quality control and product recall.

---

## Expiry Date

Expiry date means the date after which a product should not be sold or used.

This is important for:

* Food
* Medicines
* Cosmetics
* Chemicals
* Packaged goods

Expired stock can become dead stock and may cause legal or safety issues.

---

## Minimum Stock Level

Minimum stock level is the lowest quantity that should be available before urgent action is needed.

Example:

```text
Minimum stock level: 10 units
Current stock: 8 units
Action: Urgent reorder required
```

---

## Maximum Stock Level

Maximum stock level is the highest quantity a business should keep.

Example:

```text
Maximum stock level: 200 units
Current stock: 250 units
Issue: Overstock
```

Maximum stock level helps avoid excess purchases and blocked cash.

---

## Inventory Aging

Inventory aging means checking how long stock has been lying unsold.

Example:

```text
0–30 days: Fresh stock
31–60 days: Normal stock
61–90 days: Slow-moving stock
90+ days: Dead stock risk
```

Inventory aging helps identify old stock before it becomes useless.

---

## Inventory and Cash Flow

Inventory is directly connected to cash flow.

When a business buys inventory, cash goes out.

When inventory is sold, cash comes back.

If inventory does not sell quickly, cash remains blocked.

Example:

```text
Purchase stock: ₹2,00,000
Sold stock: ₹80,000
Unsold stock: ₹1,20,000
```

The business still has goods, but not enough cash.

---

## Common Inventory Problems in MSMEs

MSMEs often face inventory problems such as:

* No proper stock records
* Manual stock counting errors
* Overstocking
* Stockouts
* Dead stock
* Supplier delays
* No reorder planning
* Poor demand forecasting
* Mixing personal and business purchases
* No SKU system
* No expiry tracking
* No inventory aging report

---

## Simple Rules for MSMEs

* Track every product with an SKU.
* Check stock regularly.
* Set reorder levels for important items.
* Avoid buying too much slow-moving stock.
* Review dead stock every month.
* Track expiry dates where needed.
* Compare stock records with physical stock.
* Keep inventory data updated.
* Use inventory reports for purchase planning.
* Do not block too much cash in unsold stock.

## Summary

Inventory is one of the most important parts of MSME finance and operations.

Good inventory management helps a business avoid stockouts, reduce dead stock, improve cash flow, and make better purchase decisions.

For MSMEs, even a simple system with SKU, current stock, reorder level, purchase price, selling price, and stock status can greatly improve business control.
