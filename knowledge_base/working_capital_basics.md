# Working Capital Basics for MSMEs

## What is Working Capital?

Working capital means the money available for running day-to-day business operations.

Formula:

```text
Working Capital = Current Assets - Current Liabilities
```

In simple words:

```text
Working Capital = Money expected soon - Money payable soon
```

A business needs working capital to pay for daily expenses such as:

* Raw material
* Stock purchases
* Salaries
* Rent
* Electricity
* Transport
* Supplier payments
* GST and taxes
* Short-term loan payments

## Current Assets

Current assets are assets that can usually become cash within one year.

Examples:

* Cash in hand
* Bank balance
* Customer receivables
* Unpaid invoices
* Inventory / stock
* Short-term deposits
* Advance paid to suppliers

Example:

If a shop has ₹50,000 cash, ₹1,20,000 unpaid customer invoices, and ₹80,000 stock:

```text
Current Assets = ₹50,000 + ₹1,20,000 + ₹80,000
Current Assets = ₹2,50,000
```

## Current Liabilities

Current liabilities are amounts the business must pay within one year.

Examples:

* Supplier dues
* Rent payable
* Salary payable
* GST payable
* Short-term business loans
* Overdraft dues
* Credit card dues
* Advance received from customers
* Utility bills payable

Example:

If a business owes ₹70,000 to suppliers, ₹20,000 in rent, and ₹30,000 GST:

```text
Current Liabilities = ₹70,000 + ₹20,000 + ₹30,000
Current Liabilities = ₹1,20,000
```

## Working Capital Example

```text
Current Assets = ₹2,50,000
Current Liabilities = ₹1,20,000

Working Capital = ₹2,50,000 - ₹1,20,000
Working Capital = ₹1,30,000
```

This means the business has ₹1,30,000 available after covering short-term obligations.

## Positive Working Capital

Positive working capital means:

```text
Current Assets > Current Liabilities
```

This is usually good because the business has enough short-term assets to pay short-term dues.

Example:

```text
Current Assets = ₹5,00,000
Current Liabilities = ₹3,00,000
Working Capital = ₹2,00,000
```

The business has a working capital surplus.

## Negative Working Capital

Negative working capital means:

```text
Current Assets < Current Liabilities
```

This can be risky because the business may not have enough cash or near-cash assets to pay upcoming dues.

Example:

```text
Current Assets = ₹2,00,000
Current Liabilities = ₹3,50,000
Working Capital = -₹1,50,000
```

The business has a working capital shortage.

## Why MSMEs Face Working Capital Problems

MSMEs often face cash shortages because cash comes in later but payments go out earlier.

Common reasons:

1. Customers delay invoice payments.
2. Stock is purchased before sales happen.
3. Suppliers demand quick payment.
4. GST and tax payments are due before customers pay.
5. Seasonal sales create uneven cash flow.
6. Too much money gets blocked in slow-moving inventory.
7. Business owners give long credit periods to customers.
8. Emergency expenses reduce available cash.
9. Loan EMIs or interest payments reduce cash balance.
10. Profit exists on paper, but cash is stuck in receivables.

The Government of India notes that TReDS has been operationalized to address delayed payments to MSMEs, and MSME Samadhaan exists for monitoring outstanding dues to MSEs.

## Important Point: Profit is Not the Same as Cash

A business can show profit but still have no cash.

Example:

A business sells goods worth ₹5,00,000 on credit.

```text
Sales = ₹5,00,000
Cost = ₹3,50,000
Profit = ₹1,50,000
Cash received = ₹0
```

The business is profitable, but it has no cash until the customer pays.

This is why MSMEs must track both:

```text
Profit
Cash flow
```

## Receivables and Working Capital

Receivables are amounts customers owe to the business.

Example:

If customers have not paid ₹2,00,000, that amount is receivable.

Receivables increase current assets, but they do not help daily operations until cash is actually collected.

High receivables can create cash shortage.

## Inventory and Working Capital

Inventory means stock held by the business.

Inventory is a current asset, but it can block cash.

Example:

If a business buys ₹3,00,000 stock and only sells ₹1,00,000, then ₹2,00,000 remains blocked in stock.

Problems caused by excess inventory:

* Less cash in bank
* More storage cost
* Risk of damage or expiry
* Slow-moving goods
* Difficulty paying suppliers

## Payables and Working Capital

Payables are amounts the business owes to suppliers or others.

Example:

If a business purchased goods worth ₹1,50,000 but has not paid the supplier, that amount is payable.

Payables are current liabilities.

Higher payables reduce net working capital.

## Working Capital Cycle

The working capital cycle shows how long cash is stuck in business operations.

Simple cycle:

```text
Cash → Inventory → Sales → Receivables → Cash
```

For MSMEs, the cycle becomes risky when:

```text
Cash goes out quickly, but cash comes back slowly.
```

Example:

1. Business pays supplier today.
2. Goods are sold after 20 days.
3. Customer pays after 45 days.

Cash is blocked for around 65 days.

## Cash Conversion Cycle

The cash conversion cycle measures how long money remains stuck before returning as cash.

Simple formula:

```text
Cash Conversion Cycle = Inventory Days + Receivable Days - Payable Days
```

Meaning:

* Inventory Days: How long stock remains unsold
* Receivable Days: How long customers take to pay
* Payable Days: How long the business takes to pay suppliers

A shorter cash conversion cycle is usually better.

## Current Ratio

Current ratio helps check short-term financial health.

Formula:

```text
Current Ratio = Current Assets / Current Liabilities
```

Example:

```text
Current Assets = ₹4,00,000
Current Liabilities = ₹2,00,000

Current Ratio = 2
```

This means the business has ₹2 of current assets for every ₹1 of current liabilities.

A very low current ratio may show liquidity risk.

A very high current ratio may also mean cash is stuck in inventory or receivables.

## Quick Ratio

Quick ratio checks whether the business can pay short-term dues without depending on inventory.

Formula:

```text
Quick Ratio = (Current Assets - Inventory) / Current Liabilities
```

This is useful because inventory may not convert into cash immediately.

## Working Capital Loan

A working capital loan is used to finance daily business needs.

It should generally be used for:

* Buying stock
* Paying suppliers
* Managing receivables gap
* Seasonal working capital needs
* Short-term operating expenses

It should not be used for:

* Personal expenses
* Long-term assets without planning
* Unnecessary expansion
* Covering repeated losses without fixing the cause

Banks may assess working capital needs using projected annual turnover. Government information from March 2025 states that computation of working capital requirements of MSE units should be minimum 20% of projected annual turnover for borrowal limits up to ₹5 crore.

## Common Working Capital Finance Options

MSMEs may use:

* Cash credit
* Overdraft
* Bill discounting
* Invoice financing
* Supplier credit
* Short-term business loan
* TReDS platform for receivable discounting

RBI explains that TReDS allows MSME sellers to receive financing against confirmed invoices or bills, and the financier receives payment from the buyer on the due date.

## Delayed Payments and MSMEs

Delayed payments are one of the biggest working capital problems for MSMEs.

When buyers delay payments:

* Cash gets stuck
* Supplier payments get delayed
* Salary payments become difficult
* Loan interest burden increases
* Business growth slows down
* Owners may take expensive short-term loans

The MSME Samadhaan portal states that the MSMED Act, 2006 contains provisions for delayed payment to Micro and Small Enterprises. It also mentions that if payment is not made within 45 days of acceptance of goods or services, the buyer is liable to pay compound interest at three times the RBI-notified bank rate.

## Warning Signs of Working Capital Trouble

A business may have working capital problems if:

* Bank balance is low even when sales are high
* Customers delay payments regularly
* Supplier dues are increasing
* GST payment is difficult
* Salary payment gets delayed
* Business uses personal money repeatedly
* Stock is high but cash is low
* Short-term loans are used again and again
* Profit is visible in reports but cash is missing
* The business cannot accept new orders due to lack of funds

## How MSMEs Can Improve Working Capital

MSMEs can improve working capital by:

1. Collecting customer payments faster.
2. Giving clear payment terms on invoices.
3. Following up before due dates.
4. Avoiding excess stock.
5. Tracking slow-moving inventory.
6. Negotiating better supplier credit.
7. Separating business and personal expenses.
8. Preparing weekly cash flow reports.
9. Using invoice financing carefully.
10. Avoiding unnecessary credit sales.
11. Checking receivables ageing regularly.
12. Keeping emergency cash reserve.
13. Matching loan repayment dates with cash inflow dates.
14. Using digital records for invoices, expenses, and payments.

## Simple MSME Example

A small electronics trader has:

```text
Cash = ₹40,000
Bank Balance = ₹60,000
Receivables = ₹2,00,000
Inventory = ₹3,00,000

Supplier Dues = ₹2,50,000
GST Payable = ₹40,000
Short-term Loan Due = ₹60,000
```

Current assets:

```text
₹40,000 + ₹60,000 + ₹2,00,000 + ₹3,00,000 = ₹6,00,000
```

Current liabilities:

```text
₹2,50,000 + ₹40,000 + ₹60,000 = ₹3,50,000
```

Working capital:

```text
₹6,00,000 - ₹3,50,000 = ₹2,50,000
```

The business has positive working capital.

But if customers delay the ₹2,00,000 receivables, the business may still face cash shortage.

## AI Assistant Rules for Working Capital Questions

When a user asks about working capital, the assistant should:

1. Identify current assets.
2. Identify current liabilities.
3. Calculate working capital.
4. Check if it is positive or negative.
5. Explain the meaning in simple language.
6. Check whether cash is stuck in receivables or inventory.
7. Suggest practical steps to improve cash flow.
8. Avoid giving final loan advice without full business data.
9. Mention that profit and cash flow are different.
10. Ask for missing numbers if calculation is not possible.
