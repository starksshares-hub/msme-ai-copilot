# Small Business Accounting Basics

## Purpose of this document

Small business accounting is the process of recording, organizing, checking, and summarizing business transactions so that the owner can understand sales, purchases, expenses, profit, cash position, money receivable, and money payable.

For an MSME, accounting is not only for tax filing. It helps answer practical business questions such as:

* How much did we sell this month?
* How much money is still unpaid by customers?
* Which suppliers do we still need to pay?
* Are we profitable or only cash-positive?
* Which expenses are increasing?
* Is stock stuck in inventory?
* Can we safely take a loan or expand?

Accounting turns daily business activity into useful financial information.

---

## Core accounting idea

Every business transaction affects at least two accounts. This is called the double-entry system.

Example:

If a business sells goods worth ₹10,000 on credit:

* Sales increases by ₹10,000
* Customer receivable increases by ₹10,000

If the customer later pays ₹10,000:

* Cash or bank balance increases by ₹10,000
* Customer receivable decreases by ₹10,000

This is why accounting is more reliable than only keeping a simple cash diary.

## Important accounting terms

### Transaction

A transaction is any business activity that has financial value.

Examples:

* Sale made to a customer
* Purchase from supplier
* Rent paid
* Salary paid
* Cash received from customer
* Loan taken from bank
* Inventory purchased
* GST paid
* Electricity bill received

Only events that affect money, assets, liabilities, income, or expenses are recorded in accounting.

---

### Account

An account is a category used to record similar transactions.

Examples:

* Cash account
* Bank account
* Sales account
* Purchase account
* Rent expense account
* Salary expense account
* Customer account
* Supplier account
* GST payable account
* Loan account
* Inventory account

Accounts help group transactions so that reports can be prepared easily.

---

### Journal entry

A journal entry is the basic accounting record of a transaction. It shows which account is debited and which account is credited.

Example:

Business pays office rent of ₹20,000 by bank.

Journal entry:

* Debit: Rent Expense ₹20,000
* Credit: Bank ₹20,000

Meaning:

* Rent expense increased
* Bank balance decreased

---

### Ledger

A ledger is a collection of accounts. It shows all transactions related to one account in one place.

Example:

A customer ledger shows:

* Invoices issued to the customer
* Payments received from the customer
* Outstanding balance
* Credit notes or adjustments

Ledgers are useful for tracking individual customers, suppliers, expenses, and bank balances.

## Debit and credit basics

Debit and credit are accounting directions. They do not simply mean good or bad.

A debit usually means:

* Asset increases
* Expense increases
* Liability decreases
* Income decreases
* Owner’s equity decreases

A credit usually means:

* Asset decreases
* Expense decreases
* Liability increases
* Income increases
* Owner’s equity increases

Simple rule:

| Account type     | Increase | Decrease |
| ---------------- | -------- | -------- |
| Assets           | Debit    | Credit   |
| Expenses         | Debit    | Credit   |
| Liabilities      | Credit   | Debit    |
| Income / Revenue | Credit   | Debit    |
| Capital / Equity | Credit   | Debit    |

Examples:

1. Cash sale of ₹5,000

   * Debit Cash ₹5,000
   * Credit Sales ₹5,000

2. Purchase of goods worth ₹8,000 on credit

   * Debit Purchases / Inventory ₹8,000
   * Credit Supplier Payable ₹8,000

3. Salary paid ₹15,000 through bank

   * Debit Salary Expense ₹15,000
   * Credit Bank ₹15,000

4. Customer pays ₹12,000 against an old invoice

   * Debit Bank ₹12,000
   * Credit Accounts Receivable ₹12,000

## Main account categories

### Assets

Assets are things owned or controlled by the business that can provide future benefit.

Examples:

* Cash
* Bank balance
* Inventory
* Machinery
* Furniture
* Computer
* Vehicles
* Money receivable from customers
* Security deposits

Assets show what the business owns.

---

### Liabilities

Liabilities are obligations or amounts the business has to pay.

Examples:

* Supplier dues
* Bank loan
* GST payable
* Salary payable
* Rent payable
* Electricity bill payable
* Customer advance received

Liabilities show what the business owes.

---

### Equity / Capital

Equity is the owner’s share in the business.

Basic formula:

Assets = Liabilities + Owner’s Equity

Owner’s equity increases when the owner invests money or when the business earns profit. It decreases when the owner withdraws money or when the business makes a loss.

---

### Income / Revenue

Income is money earned from business activity.

Examples:

* Sale of goods
* Service income
* Commission income
* Interest income
* Scrap sale income

For most MSMEs, sales revenue is the main income source.

---

### Expenses

Expenses are costs incurred to run the business.

Examples:

* Rent
* Salary
* Electricity
* Internet
* Transport
* Packaging
* Repairs
* Marketing
* Bank charges
* Accounting fees
* Software subscriptions

Expenses reduce profit.

---

## Sales accounting

Sales means goods or services sold to customers.

Sales can be:

1. Cash sales
   Customer pays immediately.

2. Credit sales
   Customer receives goods/services now but pays later.

3. Online sales
   Payment may come through payment gateway, UPI, card, marketplace, or bank transfer.

4. GST sales
   If registered under GST, invoice must include tax details where applicable.

Example: Cash sale

Business sells goods worth ₹10,000 and receives cash immediately.

Entry:

* Debit Cash ₹10,000
* Credit Sales ₹10,000

Example: Credit sale

Business sells goods worth ₹10,000 to a customer on 15-day credit.

Entry:

* Debit Customer / Accounts Receivable ₹10,000
* Credit Sales ₹10,000

When payment is received:

* Debit Bank ₹10,000
* Credit Customer / Accounts Receivable ₹10,000

---

## Purchase accounting

Purchases are goods or services bought for business use.

Purchases can include:

* Raw materials
* Trading goods
* Packaging material
* Office supplies
* Business services
* Repair services
* Software tools
* Transport services

Example: Credit purchase

Business buys stock worth ₹25,000 from supplier on credit.

Entry:

* Debit Purchases / Inventory ₹25,000
* Credit Supplier / Accounts Payable ₹25,000

When supplier is paid:

* Debit Supplier / Accounts Payable ₹25,000
* Credit Bank ₹25,000

## Expenses accounting

Expenses should be recorded under correct categories. This helps the owner understand where money is going.

Common MSME expense categories:

* Rent
* Salary and wages
* Electricity
* Internet and phone
* Transport and delivery
* Repairs and maintenance
* Packaging
* Raw material
* Office supplies
* Marketing and advertising
* Bank charges
* Loan interest
* Accounting and legal fees
* Software subscription
* Fuel
* Travel
* Insurance

Example:

Business pays electricity bill of ₹4,000 by UPI.

Entry:

* Debit Electricity Expense ₹4,000
* Credit Bank ₹4,000

Expense tracking helps identify cost leakage and unnecessary spending.

## Accounts receivable

Accounts receivable means money customers owe to the business.

It is created when a business sells goods or services on credit.

Example:

A business issues invoice of ₹50,000 to a customer. The customer has not paid yet.

This ₹50,000 is accounts receivable.

Why it matters:

* High receivables mean money is stuck with customers.
* Sales may look good, but cash may be low.
* Late customer payments can create working capital problems.
* Receivable ageing helps identify overdue invoices.

Important receivable fields:

* Customer name
* Invoice number
* Invoice date
* Due date
* Invoice amount
* Amount received
* Balance due
* Days overdue
* Payment status

Common receivable statuses:

* Unpaid
* Partially paid
* Paid
* Overdue
* Disputed
* Written off

---

## Accounts payable

Accounts payable means money the business owes to suppliers or vendors.

It is created when a business buys goods or services on credit.

Example:

A business purchases raw material worth ₹30,000 from a supplier and will pay after 20 days.

This ₹30,000 is accounts payable.

Why it matters:

* Payables help the business manage cash flow.
* Paying too early may reduce available cash.
* Paying too late may damage supplier relationships.
* Supplier due dates should be tracked carefully.

Important payable fields:

* Supplier name
* Bill number
* Bill date
* Due date
* Bill amount
* Amount paid
* Balance due
* Payment status

Common payable statuses:

* Unpaid
* Partially paid
* Paid
* Overdue
* On hold
* Disputed

## Cash basis vs accrual basis

### Cash basis accounting

In cash basis accounting, income and expenses are recorded only when money is received or paid.

Example:

If an invoice is issued in January but payment is received in February, income is recorded in February.

Cash basis is simple, but it can hide unpaid invoices and pending bills.

---

### Accrual basis accounting

In accrual basis accounting, income is recorded when earned, and expenses are recorded when incurred, even if cash is not yet received or paid.

Example:

If an invoice is issued in January, income is recorded in January even if payment comes in February.

Accrual accounting gives a better picture of profit and business performance. ICAI’s Accounting Standard 1 identifies going concern, consistency, and accrual as fundamental accounting assumptions for financial statements.

For MSME decision-making, accrual records are useful because they show both:

* Actual profit
* Pending receivables and payables

---

## Profit vs cash

Profit and cash are not the same.

A business can show profit but still have low cash if customers have not paid.

Example:

Sales: ₹1,00,000
Expenses: ₹70,000
Profit: ₹30,000

But if customers paid only ₹20,000 and the rest is unpaid, the business may still face cash shortage.

This is common in MSMEs.

Reasons profit and cash differ:

* Credit sales not yet collected
* Supplier payments due soon
* Inventory bought but not sold
* Loan EMI paid from cash
* Owner withdrawals
* GST or tax payments due
* Advance payments received or paid

The AI copilot should not assume that profit means enough cash is available.

## Inventory accounting

Inventory means goods held for sale or production.

Examples:

* Raw material
* Finished goods
* Trading stock
* Packaging material
* Spare parts
* Work-in-progress

Inventory affects both profit and cash.

If stock is purchased but not sold, cash goes out but profit is not earned yet.

For GST-registered businesses, records of stock, goods received, goods supplied, goods lost, stolen, destroyed, written off, or disposed as gift/free sample may need to be maintained under GST rules.

Important inventory fields:

* Item name
* SKU/code
* Opening stock
* Purchases
* Sales/usage
* Closing stock
* Purchase cost
* Selling price
* Reorder level
* Damaged/expired stock
* Slow-moving stock

---

## GST-aware accounting

GST affects accounting because invoices, purchases, tax collected, and input tax credit must be tracked properly.

For a GST-registered business, records may include:

* Inward supplies
* Outward supplies
* Stock of goods
* Input tax credit availed
* Output tax payable and paid
* Other prescribed records

The CGST Act requires registered persons to keep true and correct accounts at the principal place of business, including records of production/manufacture, inward and outward supplies, stock, input tax credit, and output tax payable/paid.

GST-related accounting accounts may include:

* Output CGST
* Output SGST
* Output IGST
* Input CGST
* Input SGST
* Input IGST
* GST payable
* GST receivable/input credit
* Reverse charge payable

Example: GST sale within same state

Taxable sale value: ₹10,000
GST rate: 18%
CGST: ₹900
SGST: ₹900
Invoice total: ₹11,800

Entry:

* Debit Customer / Bank ₹11,800
* Credit Sales ₹10,000
* Credit Output CGST ₹900
* Credit Output SGST ₹900

Example: GST purchase within same state

Taxable purchase value: ₹5,000
GST rate: 18%
CGST: ₹450
SGST: ₹450
Bill total: ₹5,900

Entry:

* Debit Purchases / Inventory ₹5,000
* Debit Input CGST ₹450
* Debit Input SGST ₹450
* Credit Supplier / Bank ₹5,900

## Tax invoice and accounting records

A tax invoice is an important accounting document because it supports sales, GST, receivables, and audit trail.

Under GST, a registered person supplying taxable goods generally issues a tax invoice before or at the time of removal or delivery of goods. For taxable services, the tax invoice is issued before or after provision of service within the prescribed period.

Important invoice-linked accounting fields:

* Invoice number
* Invoice date
* Customer name
* Customer GSTIN, if applicable
* Item description
* Quantity
* Rate
* Taxable value
* GST rate
* CGST/SGST/IGST amount
* Total invoice value
* Payment terms
* Due date
* Payment status

A good accounting system should connect invoice data with receivables, GST records, sales reports, and cash flow.

## Basic financial reports

Small business accounting should produce useful reports.

### Sales report

Shows total sales by day, month, customer, product, or category.

Useful questions:

* Which month had highest sales?
* Which customer buys the most?
* Which product sells fastest?
* Are sales increasing or decreasing?

---

### Expense report

Shows money spent by category.

Useful questions:

* Which expense category is highest?
* Did rent, salary, or transport increase?
* Are marketing expenses giving returns?
* Are any costs unusually high?

---

### Accounts receivable report

Shows unpaid customer invoices.

Useful questions:

* Which customers have not paid?
* Which invoices are overdue?
* How much money is stuck in receivables?
* Which customer has the highest outstanding balance?

---

### Accounts payable report

Shows unpaid supplier bills.

Useful questions:

* Which supplier needs to be paid soon?
* What bills are overdue?
* How much cash is needed this week?
* Can payment be delayed without risk?

---

### Profit and loss statement

Shows income, expenses, and profit/loss for a period.

Basic formula:

Revenue - Expenses = Profit or Loss

A P&L helps understand business performance.

---

### Balance sheet

Shows assets, liabilities, and owner’s equity at a point in time.

Basic formula:

Assets = Liabilities + Equity

A balance sheet helps understand financial position.

---

### Cash flow report

Shows cash inflow and outflow.

Important categories:

* Cash from customers
* Cash paid to suppliers
* Cash paid for expenses
* Loan received
* EMI paid
* Owner investment
* Owner withdrawal

Cash flow is critical for MSMEs because many businesses fail due to cash shortage, even when sales are happening.

## Common MSME accounting mistakes

1. Mixing personal and business expenses
   This makes profit calculation inaccurate.

2. Not recording credit sales
   The owner may forget money that customers still owe.

3. Not tracking supplier dues
   Bills may become overdue and damage relationships.

4. Recording only cash, not invoices
   This hides receivables and payables.

5. Not matching bank entries with invoices
   Payments may remain unidentified.

6. Ignoring small expenses
   Small daily expenses can become large monthly leakage.

7. Not recording GST separately
   GST collected is not business income. It may be payable to the government after adjustment.

8. Not maintaining stock records
   The owner cannot know whether stock is sold, damaged, stolen, or stuck.

9. Not checking duplicate invoices
   Duplicate entries can overstate expenses or sales.

10. Not checking total mismatches
    Invoice subtotal, tax, and total should match.

11. Not keeping proper source documents
    Missing bills and invoices create problems during verification, tax filing, or audit.

## Accounting rules the AI copilot should understand

The AI assistant should understand these rules:

1. Sales increase income.
2. Credit sales create accounts receivable.
3. Customer payments reduce accounts receivable.
4. Purchases increase inventory or expense.
5. Credit purchases create accounts payable.
6. Supplier payments reduce accounts payable.
7. Expenses reduce profit.
8. GST collected is a liability, not revenue.
9. Input GST may be treated separately from purchase cost where input tax credit is available.
10. Cash received is not always income.
11. Cash paid is not always expense.
12. Loan received increases cash and liability.
13. EMI payment may include principal and interest.
14. Owner investment increases cash and equity.
15. Owner withdrawal reduces cash and equity.
16. Profit and cash can be different.
17. Inventory purchase affects cash immediately but profit only when goods are sold.
18. Receivables and payables are important for working capital.
19. Every transaction should have a date, amount, party, category, and source document.
20. Accounting answers should mention uncertainty if required data is missing.

## Example business scenarios

### Scenario 1: Sale on credit

A business sells goods worth ₹20,000 to Rahul Traders on 10-day credit.

Accounting meaning:

* Sales increased
* Rahul Traders owes money
* Cash has not increased yet

AI answer should say:

This is a credit sale. It increases sales and accounts receivable. It does not improve cash until Rahul Traders pays.

---

### Scenario 2: Customer payment received

Rahul Traders pays ₹20,000 by bank transfer.

Accounting meaning:

* Bank balance increased
* Receivable from Rahul Traders decreased
* No new sale happened

AI answer should say:

This is collection of an old receivable, not new revenue.

---

### Scenario 3: Supplier bill unpaid

Business purchases stock worth ₹40,000 from a supplier and has not paid yet.

Accounting meaning:

* Inventory/purchase increased
* Supplier payable increased
* Cash has not gone out yet

AI answer should say:

This purchase creates a payable. It will affect cash flow when payment is made.

---

### Scenario 4: Rent paid

Business pays rent of ₹15,000.

Accounting meaning:

* Rent expense increased
* Bank/cash decreased
* Profit decreased

AI answer should say:

Rent is an operating expense and reduces profit.

---

### Scenario 5: Loan received

Business receives bank loan of ₹2,00,000.

Accounting meaning:

* Bank balance increased
* Loan liability increased
* It is not income

AI answer should say:

Loan received improves cash but does not count as revenue or profit.

---

### Scenario 6: GST collected on sale

Business sells goods for ₹10,000 plus 18% GST.

Accounting meaning:

* Sales revenue is ₹10,000
* GST collected is ₹1,800
* Total invoice is ₹11,800
* GST collected is not business income

AI answer should say:

Only the taxable value is sales income. GST collected should be tracked as tax liability, subject to input tax credit adjustment and GST rules.

## Short summary

Small business accounting records daily business transactions and converts them into useful financial information. It helps MSMEs track sales, expenses, cash, customer dues, supplier dues, GST, inventory, profit, and business performance. A good accounting system should not only store transactions but also help the owner make better decisions about cash flow, payments, expenses, and growth.
