Definitional Framework and Historical Evolution of MSMEsThe Micro, Small, and Medium Enterprises (MSME) sector is widely recognized as the structural backbone of India's industrial economy, serving as a primary driver of domestic production, employment generation, and grassroots entrepreneurship. Formally established under the Micro, Small and Medium Enterprises Development (MSMED) Act, 2006, these enterprises encompass business entities engaged in the manufacturing, production, processing, or preservation of goods, as well as those engaged in the delivery or rendering of services.Historically, the identification and regulation of MSMEs in India were governed by highly segmented criteria. Under the legacy framework of the MSMED Act, 2006, manufacturing and service enterprises were classified under completely separate and non-fungible investment thresholds. Manufacturing entities were classified based strictly on their investment in plant and machinery, while service-oriented firms were categorized based on their investment in equipment.The table below outlines this legacy structural matrix, which created significant regulatory silos:Pre-2020 Sector-Specific MSME Classification ThresholdsEnterprise CategoryManufacturing Sector: Investment in Plant & MachineryServices Sector: Investment in EquipmentMicro Enterprise$\le$ ₹25 Lakh$\le$ ₹10 LakhSmall Enterprise> ₹25 Lakh but < ₹5 Crore> ₹10 Lakh but < ₹2 CroreMedium Enterprise> ₹5 Crore but < ₹10 Crore> ₹2 Crore but < ₹5 CroreThis bifurcated model placed strict ceilings on capital-intensive manufacturing units, which frequently discouraged small businesses from upgrading their machinery for fear of breaching the investment limits and losing their associated regulatory benefits. Recognizing these bottlenecks, the central government enacted a major structural reform in July 2020. This reform eliminated the distinction between the manufacturing and services sectors, introducing a unified "composite criterion" that integrated both investment in plant and machinery (or equipment) and annual turnover.The composite framework was designed to ensure that both parameters must be met to remain within a specific category, thereby establishing a more flexible and holistic regulatory environment. As the Indian economy targeted broader global value chain integration, the 2020 thresholds became restrictive for high-growth enterprises.Consequently, the Union Budget 2025-26 introduced a major revision to the composite MSME classification limits, raising investment ceilings by 2.5 times and doubling the turnover thresholds, effective from April 1, 2025.
