# ITR-4 for Small Businesses

## Short Description

ITR-4, also called **Sugam**, is an income tax return form used by eligible small taxpayers who report business or professional income under the **presumptive taxation scheme**. It is commonly relevant for small shopkeepers, traders, freelancers, service providers, consultants, and small transport businesses that do not want to maintain full detailed books like larger businesses.

For AY 2026-27, ITR-4 can generally be filed by a resident individual, HUF, or firm other than LLP whose income does not exceed ₹50 lakh and whose business or professional income is computed on a presumptive basis under sections 44AD, 44ADA, or 44AE.

---

## What Is ITR-4?

ITR-4 is an income tax return form for small taxpayers who declare income using simplified rules instead of calculating exact profit by deducting every business expense.

In normal business accounting:

```text
Profit = Sales - Expenses
```

In presumptive taxation:

```text
Taxable business income = Prescribed percentage of turnover / receipts
```

This makes tax filing simpler for small businesses because they do not need to prepare a full profit and loss account in the same detailed way as regular businesses.

## Who Commonly Uses ITR-4?

ITR-4 is commonly used by:

* Small shop owners
* Local traders
* Wholesalers and retailers
* Small service providers
* Freelancers using presumptive taxation
* Professionals eligible under Section 44ADA
* Small partnership firms other than LLPs
* Small transport operators covered under Section 44AE
* Individuals with salary plus small business income
* Individuals with business income plus interest income

Example users:

```text
A grocery shop owner with ₹35 lakh turnover may use ITR-4 if eligible under presumptive taxation.
```

```text
A freelance designer or consultant may use ITR-4 if eligible under Section 44ADA.
```

```text
A small trader using digital payments and declaring presumptive profit may use ITR-4.
```

## Who Can File ITR-4?

A taxpayer may generally file ITR-4 if they are:

* A resident individual
* A resident Hindu Undivided Family, also called HUF
* A firm other than LLP
* Having total income up to ₹50 lakh
* Having business or professional income under presumptive taxation
* Having income from salary or pension
* Having income from up to two house properties
* Having agricultural income up to ₹5,000
* Having income from other sources such as savings interest, deposit interest, income tax refund interest, or family pension

The Income Tax Department’s AY 2026-27 FAQ lists these eligibility conditions for ITR-4.

---

## Who Should Not File ITR-4?

ITR-4 is not suitable for every business owner. A taxpayer should not use ITR-4 if they are not covered by the eligibility rules.

A taxpayer generally cannot use ITR-4 if they:

* Are non-resident or resident but not ordinarily resident
* Have total income above ₹50 lakh
* Have short-term capital gains
* Have long-term capital gains under Section 112A above ₹1.25 lakh
* Have agricultural income above ₹5,000
* Are a director in a company
* Have income from more than two house properties
* Have lottery income or racehorse income
* Held unlisted equity shares during the previous year
* Are not eligible for the presumptive taxation scheme

The Income Tax Department lists these as cases where ITR-4 cannot be filed for AY 2026-27.

## Presumptive Taxation Meaning

Presumptive taxation is a simplified tax system for small taxpayers. Instead of calculating exact profit by recording every expense, the law assumes a minimum profit based on turnover or gross receipts.

The Income Tax Department explains that presumptive taxation under sections 44AD, 44ADA, and 44AE is meant to reduce the compliance burden for small taxpayers who would otherwise need to maintain regular books of accounts under certain circumstances.

---

## Main Presumptive Tax Sections

### Section 44AD — Small Business

Section 44AD applies to eligible small businesses. It is commonly used by traders, shopkeepers, and small business owners.

Under Section 44AD, presumptive income is generally calculated at:

```text
6% of eligible digital / banking receipts
8% of cash or non-digital receipts
```

The Income Tax Department explains that income under Section 44AD is computed at 8% of turnover or gross receipts, and 6% for receipts through account payee cheque, bank draft, electronic clearing system, or prescribed electronic mode.

The turnover limit under Section 44AD is generally ₹2 crore, but it can go up to ₹3 crore where cash receipts do not exceed 5% of total gross receipts.

Example:

```text
Business turnover through digital payments = ₹20,00,000
Minimum presumptive income at 6% = ₹1,20,000
```

```text
Business turnover in cash = ₹10,00,000
Minimum presumptive income at 8% = ₹80,000
```

---

### Section 44ADA — Professionals

Section 44ADA applies to eligible professionals such as:

* Legal professionals
* Medical professionals
* Engineering professionals
* Architectural professionals
* Accountants
* Technical consultants
* Interior decorators
* Other notified professionals

For Section 44ADA, the gross receipts limit is generally ₹50 lakh, but it can go up to ₹75 lakh if cash receipts do not exceed 5% of total gross receipts.

Under Section 44ADA, presumptive income is generally declared at 50% of gross receipts. The Income Tax Department states that specified professionals opting for Section 44ADA and declaring income at 50% of gross receipts are not required to maintain books of account for that specified profession under Section 44AA.

Example:

```text
Professional gross receipts = ₹12,00,000
Presumptive income at 50% = ₹6,00,000
```

---

### Section 44AE — Goods Carriage Business

Section 44AE applies to taxpayers engaged in the business of plying, hiring, or leasing goods carriages.

It is relevant for small transport operators who own not more than ten goods carriages during the previous year.

Example:

```text
A small transporter with goods vehicles may use Section 44AE if eligible.
```

## Records Needed for ITR-4

Even though presumptive taxation reduces bookkeeping burden, the taxpayer should still keep important records.

Useful records include:

* PAN and Aadhaar details
* Bank statements
* Sales or turnover records
* Cash receipts summary
* Digital payment receipts
* GST return data, if registered under GST
* Form 26AS
* AIS
* Form 16, if salary income exists
* Form 16A, if TDS is deducted
* Housing loan interest certificate, if claiming house property interest
* Rent receipts or rental agreement, if applicable
* Donation receipts
* Investment and insurance premium receipts

The Income Tax Department lists Form 16, Form 16A, Form 26AS, AIS, payslip, bank statements, housing loan interest certificate, rent receipts, rental agreement, donation receipts, and investment or premium payment receipts as documents that may be needed for ITR-4 filing.

## Why Small Businesses Use ITR-4

Small businesses use ITR-4 because it can reduce compliance complexity.

Main benefits:

* Simpler tax filing
* Less detailed bookkeeping requirement
* Easier profit calculation
* Useful for small traders and professionals
* Helps report business income legally
* Helps match income with bank records, GST records, and TDS records
* Useful for loan applications because filed ITR works as income proof

Example:

```text
A small shop owner may not maintain detailed profit and loss books.
Using presumptive taxation, they can declare profit as a fixed percentage of turnover.
```

## ITR-4 and GST

ITR-4 is an income tax return. GST returns are separate.

A business may need both:

```text
GST return = Reports sales, purchases, output tax, input tax credit
ITR-4 = Reports income, presumptive profit, deductions, and tax payable
```

For a GST-registered small business, turnover shown in GST returns should broadly match the sales or gross receipts reported in income tax records.

Mismatch example:

```text
GST sales reported = ₹42,00,000
Business turnover in ITR-4 = ₹25,00,000
```

This mismatch may create questions unless there is a valid reason.

---

## Common Mistakes in ITR-4

Common ITR-4 mistakes include:

* Using ITR-4 even when not eligible
* Reporting wrong turnover
* Ignoring cash and digital receipt split
* Not matching turnover with GST records
* Forgetting bank interest income
* Ignoring TDS shown in Form 26AS or AIS
* Filing under wrong tax regime
* Not filing Form 10-IEA when needed for old regime
* Reporting income below presumptive rate without understanding audit consequences
* Forgetting to verify the return after filing

For taxpayers with business income, the Income Tax Department states that choosing the old tax regime requires filing Form 10-IEA before the due date under Section 139(1), and such taxpayers cannot freely switch between old and new regimes every year.

---

## Due Date

For AY 2026-27, the Income Tax Department FAQ states that the due date for filing ITR-4 for FY 2025-26 is **31 August 2026**.

## Simple Example

### Case

Ravi runs a small mobile accessories shop.

```text
Annual turnover = ₹30,00,000
Digital receipts = ₹20,00,000
Cash receipts = ₹10,00,000
```

### Presumptive income calculation

```text
6% of ₹20,00,000 = ₹1,20,000
8% of ₹10,00,000 = ₹80,000
Total presumptive income = ₹2,00,000
```

### Interpretation

Ravi may report ₹2,00,000 as presumptive business income if he is eligible. He should still keep bank records, digital payment records, cash sales summary, GST data if applicable, and tax documents.
