# Balance Sheet Basics for MSMEs

## Purpose of this file

This file explains the basic concepts of a balance sheet for micro, small, and medium enterprises. It is written for simple business finance understanding and for use in a RAG knowledge base.

A balance sheet helps a business owner understand what the business owns, what the business owes, and what amount belongs to the owner after debts are considered.

The three main parts of a balance sheet are:

1. Assets
2. Liabilities
3. Equity

The basic balance sheet equation is:

```text
Assets = Liabilities + Equity
```

This means everything owned by the business is funded either by borrowing money, delaying payments, or by the owner’s own capital/profit.

---

## What is a balance sheet?

A balance sheet is a financial statement that shows the financial position of a business on a specific date.

Unlike a profit and loss statement, which shows performance over a period, a balance sheet shows the position at one point in time.

Example:

```text
Balance Sheet as on 31 March 2026
```

This means the statement shows what the business owns and owes on that exact date.

A balance sheet answers questions like:

* How much cash does the business have?
* How much money is stuck in inventory?
* How much money do customers still owe?
* How much does the business owe to suppliers?
* How much loan is outstanding?
* How much capital belongs to the owner?
* Is the business financially stable?

---

## Why balance sheet matters for MSMEs

For MSMEs, the balance sheet is important because many small businesses struggle with cash visibility.

A business can show profit but still have poor cash flow. This often happens when money is stuck in receivables, inventory, or unpaid bills.

The balance sheet helps the owner understand:

* Whether the business has enough short-term assets to pay short-term liabilities
* Whether customers are delaying payments
* Whether inventory is too high
* Whether loans are becoming too heavy
* Whether the owner’s capital is increasing or decreasing
* Whether the business is growing safely or becoming risky

A balance sheet is also useful when applying for loans, checking business health, preparing reports, and making financial decisions.

---

## Balance sheet equation

The main formula of a balance sheet is:

```text
Assets = Liabilities + Equity
```

This formula must always balance.

Another way to understand it:

```text
What the business owns = What the business owes + What belongs to the owner
```

Example:

```text
Assets = ₹10,00,000
Liabilities = ₹6,00,000
Equity = ₹4,00,000

₹10,00,000 = ₹6,00,000 + ₹4,00,000
```

This means the business owns assets worth ₹10,00,000. Out of this, ₹6,00,000 is funded by outsiders such as banks, lenders, and suppliers. The remaining ₹4,00,000 belongs to the owner.

---

## Assets

Assets are things owned or controlled by the business that have economic value.

Assets can help the business earn income, run operations, or settle obligations.

Common MSME assets include:

* Cash in hand
* Bank balance
* Inventory
* Customer receivables
* Machinery
* Furniture
* Computers
* Vehicles
* Security deposits
* Office equipment
* Land or building, if owned by the business

Assets are usually divided into two types:

1. Current assets
2. Non-current assets

---

## Current assets

Current assets are assets that are expected to be converted into cash, sold, consumed, or used within one year or within the normal operating cycle of the business.

For MSMEs, current assets are very important because they directly affect daily cash flow.

Common current assets include:

* Cash
* Bank balance
* Accounts receivable
* Inventory
* Short-term deposits
* Prepaid expenses
* Advances paid to suppliers

Example:

```text
Cash in hand: ₹20,000
Bank balance: ₹1,50,000
Inventory: ₹3,00,000
Customer receivables: ₹2,50,000
Total current assets: ₹7,20,000
```

Current assets help answer:

```text
Can the business pay its short-term bills?
```

---

## Cash and bank balance

Cash and bank balance are the most liquid assets of a business.

Cash includes physical cash available with the business.

Bank balance includes money available in current accounts, savings accounts, or business bank accounts.

Example:

```text
Cash in hand: ₹15,000
Bank balance: ₹85,000
Total cash and bank balance: ₹1,00,000
```

Cash is important because it is immediately usable for payments such as:

* Rent
* Salary
* Supplier payments
* Electricity bills
* Transport expenses
* Emergency purchases

A profitable business can still face problems if it does not have enough cash.

---

## Accounts receivable

Accounts receivable means money that customers owe to the business.

It usually happens when goods or services are sold on credit.

Example:

```text
A business sells goods worth ₹50,000 to a customer.
The customer agrees to pay after 30 days.

Until payment is received, ₹50,000 is recorded as accounts receivable.
```

Receivables are assets because the business has a right to receive money.

For MSMEs, receivables are very important because delayed customer payments can create cash shortages.

High receivables may mean:

* Sales are happening on credit
* Customers are delaying payments
* The business has weak collection follow-up
* Cash is stuck even though revenue has been recorded

---

## Inventory

Inventory means goods or materials held by the business for sale, production, or use.

Common types of inventory include:

* Raw materials
* Work-in-progress goods
* Finished goods
* Trading stock
* Spare parts
* Packing material

Example:

```text
A shop has products worth ₹2,00,000 lying in stock.
This stock is recorded as inventory under assets.
```

Inventory is an asset because it can be sold to generate revenue.

However, too much inventory can create problems. Money gets blocked in stock instead of being available as cash.

Inventory problems for MSMEs include:

* Dead stock
* Slow-moving stock
* Overstocking
* Stock damage
* Stock theft
* Poor stock records
* Understocking of fast-moving items

Inventory should be monitored regularly because it directly affects working capital.

---

## Prepaid expenses

Prepaid expenses are payments made in advance for benefits that will be received in the future.

Example:

```text
A business pays ₹60,000 rent in advance for 6 months.
At the time of payment, it is not fully an expense.
The unused portion is shown as a prepaid expense asset.
```

Common prepaid expenses include:

* Advance rent
* Advance insurance
* Annual software subscription
* Advance maintenance charges

Prepaid expenses are current assets if the benefit will be used within one year.

---

## Advances paid to suppliers

Advances paid to suppliers are amounts paid before goods or services are received.

Example:

```text
A business pays ₹30,000 advance to a supplier for raw materials.
The supplier has not delivered the goods yet.
The advance is shown as an asset.
```

This is an asset because the business will either receive goods or get the money back.

For MSMEs, supplier advances should be tracked carefully to avoid forgotten payments or disputes.

---

## Non-current assets

Non-current assets are long-term assets used in the business for more than one year.

They are not expected to be converted into cash quickly.

Common non-current assets include:

* Machinery
* Vehicles
* Computers
* Furniture
* Office equipment
* Factory equipment
* Land
* Building
* Long-term deposits

Example:

```text
A business buys machinery for ₹5,00,000.
The machinery will be used for several years.
It is recorded as a non-current asset.
```

Non-current assets help the business operate and generate revenue over a long period.

---

## Fixed assets

Fixed assets are long-term physical assets used to run the business.

Examples:

* Machines
* Delivery vehicles
* Furniture
* Computers
* Tools
* Factory equipment
* Office equipment

Fixed assets are not bought for resale. They are bought for business use.

Example:

```text
A printing business buys a printing machine.
The machine is a fixed asset because it helps the business produce printed materials.
```

Fixed assets usually lose value over time due to usage, wear and tear, or age. This reduction in value is called depreciation.

---

## Depreciation

Depreciation means spreading the cost of a fixed asset over its useful life.

Example:

```text
A machine costs ₹1,00,000.
The business expects to use it for 5 years.

Instead of recording the full ₹1,00,000 as expense in one year, the cost may be spread over 5 years.
```

Depreciation helps show that assets lose value over time.

Depreciation affects:

* Profit and loss statement, because depreciation is an expense
* Balance sheet, because the asset value reduces over time

Simple example:

```text
Machine cost: ₹1,00,000
Depreciation for the year: ₹20,000
Book value after one year: ₹80,000
```

Book value means the value of the asset shown in accounting records after depreciation.

---

## Liabilities

Liabilities are amounts the business owes to others.

Liabilities represent obligations that the business must pay in the future.

Common MSME liabilities include:

* Supplier dues
* Business loans
* GST payable
* Salary payable
* Rent payable
* Electricity bill payable
* Bank overdraft
* Credit card dues
* Customer advances
* EMI payable

Liabilities are divided into two main types:

1. Current liabilities
2. Non-current liabilities

---

## Current liabilities

Current liabilities are obligations that are expected to be paid within one year or within the normal operating cycle of the business.

Common current liabilities include:

* Accounts payable
* Short-term loans
* GST payable
* Salary payable
* Rent payable
* Electricity payable
* Outstanding expenses
* Customer advances
* Current portion of long-term loans

Example:

```text
Supplier payable: ₹1,20,000
GST payable: ₹40,000
Salary payable: ₹60,000
Short-term loan: ₹1,00,000
Total current liabilities: ₹3,20,000
```

Current liabilities are important because they show how much the business needs to pay soon.

---

## Accounts payable

Accounts payable means money the business owes to suppliers or vendors.

It usually happens when goods or services are purchased on credit.

Example:

```text
A business buys goods worth ₹70,000 from a supplier.
The supplier allows payment after 30 days.

Until payment is made, ₹70,000 is recorded as accounts payable.
```

Accounts payable is a liability because the business must pay this amount later.

For MSMEs, supplier payables must be managed carefully. Delaying supplier payments too much can damage relationships and affect supply continuity.

---

## Creditors

Creditors are people or businesses to whom the business owes money.

In MSME accounting, creditors usually refer to suppliers or vendors who provided goods or services on credit.

Example:

```text
ABC Traders supplies goods worth ₹25,000.
Payment is not yet made.

ABC Traders is a creditor.
₹25,000 is shown as liability.
```

Creditors are important because they show unpaid business obligations.

High creditors may mean:

* The business is buying heavily on credit
* Cash flow is tight
* Supplier payments are delayed
* The business is using supplier credit as working capital

---

## Loans

Loans are borrowed amounts that the business must repay.

Loans can be taken from:

* Banks
* NBFCs
* Financial institutions
* Owners
* Friends or family
* Private lenders

Loans may be short-term or long-term.

Example:

```text
Business loan from bank: ₹5,00,000
Amount repaid: ₹1,00,000
Outstanding loan: ₹4,00,000
```

The outstanding amount is shown as a liability in the balance sheet.

Loans usually come with interest. Interest expense is shown in the profit and loss statement, while the outstanding loan balance is shown in the balance sheet.

---

## Short-term loans

Short-term loans are loans that must be repaid within one year.

Examples:

* Working capital loan
* Short-term business loan
* Bank overdraft
* Cash credit facility
* Short-term borrowing from owner
* Temporary borrowing from relatives or partners

Short-term loans help businesses handle immediate cash needs.

Example:

```text
A business takes ₹2,00,000 as a 6-month working capital loan.
This is a current liability.
```

Short-term loans should be monitored because repayment pressure comes quickly.

---

## Long-term loans

Long-term loans are loans repayable over more than one year.

Examples:

* Machinery loan
* Vehicle loan
* Business expansion loan
* Term loan
* Property loan

Example:

```text
A business takes a ₹10,00,000 machinery loan repayable over 5 years.
The long-term portion is shown under non-current liabilities.
The amount due within the next 12 months is shown as current liability.
```

Long-term loans can help a business grow, but too much debt can create financial risk.

---

## GST payable and tax liabilities

GST payable means GST collected from customers that needs to be paid to the government after adjusting eligible input tax credit.

Example:

```text
GST collected on sales: ₹50,000
Input GST paid on purchases: ₹30,000
Net GST payable: ₹20,000
```

The payable amount is shown as a current liability until paid.

Other tax-related liabilities may include:

* TDS payable
* Income tax payable
* Professional tax payable
* Provident fund payable, if applicable

Tax liabilities should be recorded separately from normal business expenses because they are statutory obligations.

---

## Outstanding expenses

Outstanding expenses are expenses that have been incurred but not yet paid.

Example:

```text
Salary for March is ₹80,000.
It will be paid in April.
As on 31 March, salary payable is shown as a liability.
```

Common outstanding expenses include:

* Salary payable
* Rent payable
* Electricity payable
* Internet bill payable
* Interest payable
* Transport payable

Outstanding expenses help show the real financial position of the business.

---

## Customer advances

Customer advances are amounts received from customers before goods or services are delivered.

Example:

```text
A customer pays ₹40,000 advance for a custom order.
The business has not delivered the order yet.
This amount is shown as a liability.
```

Customer advance is a liability because the business must either deliver the goods/services or refund the money.

For MSMEs, customer advances are useful for cash flow, but they should not be treated as final income until the obligation is fulfilled.

---

## Non-current liabilities

Non-current liabilities are obligations payable after more than one year.

Examples:

* Long-term business loans
* Machinery loans
* Vehicle loans
* Long-term lease obligations
* Long-term borrowing from owner or partners

Example:

```text
A business has a term loan of ₹8,00,000.
₹2,00,000 is due within one year.
₹6,00,000 is due after one year.

Current liability: ₹2,00,000
Non-current liability: ₹6,00,000
```

Non-current liabilities show long-term financial commitments.

---

## Equity

Equity represents the owner’s claim in the business after all liabilities are deducted from assets.

Formula:

```text
Equity = Assets - Liabilities
```

Example:

```text
Assets: ₹12,00,000
Liabilities: ₹7,00,000
Equity: ₹5,00,000
```

This means the owner’s net claim in the business is ₹5,00,000.

Equity may include:

* Owner’s capital
* Partner capital
* Retained earnings
* Current year profit
* Less: drawings or withdrawals

---

## Owner’s capital

Owner’s capital is the money invested by the owner into the business.

Example:

```text
The owner starts a business with ₹3,00,000.
This amount is recorded as owner’s capital.
```

Owner’s capital increases when:

* Owner invests more money
* Business earns profit

Owner’s capital decreases when:

* Owner withdraws money
* Business makes losses

---

## Drawings

Drawings are amounts withdrawn by the owner from the business for personal use.

Example:

```text
Owner withdraws ₹20,000 from the business bank account for personal expenses.
This is recorded as drawings.
```

Drawings reduce owner’s equity.

Drawings are not business expenses. They should not be recorded as salary, rent, or operational cost unless there is a proper business reason.

For MSMEs, separating business money and personal money is very important.

---

## Retained earnings

Retained earnings are profits kept in the business instead of being withdrawn by the owner.

Example:

```text
Business earns profit of ₹2,00,000.
Owner withdraws ₹50,000.
Retained earnings: ₹1,50,000
```

Retained earnings increase equity and help fund future growth.

A business with strong retained earnings may be more financially stable because it is using its own profits to grow.

---

## Current assets vs current liabilities

Current assets and current liabilities are compared to understand short-term financial health.

Formula:

```text
Working Capital = Current Assets - Current Liabilities
```

Example:

```text
Current assets: ₹8,00,000
Current liabilities: ₹5,00,000
Working capital: ₹3,00,000
```

Positive working capital means the business has more short-term assets than short-term obligations.

Negative working capital means the business may struggle to pay short-term dues.

---

## Working capital connection

Working capital is closely connected to the balance sheet.

Current assets such as cash, receivables, and inventory increase working capital.

Current liabilities such as supplier dues, unpaid bills, and short-term loans reduce working capital.

Example:

```text
Cash: ₹1,00,000
Receivables: ₹3,00,000
Inventory: ₹4,00,000
Current assets: ₹8,00,000

Supplier payable: ₹2,50,000
GST payable: ₹50,000
Short-term loan: ₹2,00,000
Current liabilities: ₹5,00,000

Working capital: ₹3,00,000
```

For MSMEs, working capital is often more important than profit because daily operations depend on available cash.

## Example simple MSME balance sheet

```text
Balance Sheet as on 31 March 2026

Assets

Current Assets:
Cash in hand                         ₹20,000
Bank balance                         ₹1,30,000
Accounts receivable                  ₹2,50,000
Inventory                            ₹3,00,000
Prepaid expenses                     ₹50,000

Total Current Assets                 ₹7,50,000

Non-Current Assets:
Machinery                            ₹4,00,000
Furniture                            ₹80,000
Computer equipment                   ₹70,000

Total Non-Current Assets             ₹5,50,000

Total Assets                         ₹13,00,000


Liabilities

Current Liabilities:
Supplier payable                     ₹2,00,000
GST payable                          ₹50,000
Salary payable                       ₹75,000
Short-term loan                      ₹1,25,000

Total Current Liabilities            ₹4,50,000

Non-Current Liabilities:
Long-term business loan              ₹3,00,000

```

In this example:

```text
Assets = ₹13,00,000
Liabilities = ₹7,50,000
Equity = ₹5,50,000

₹13,00,000 = ₹7,50,000 + ₹5,50,000
```

The balance sheet balances correctly.

## How to read a balance sheet quickly

A business owner can read a balance sheet by checking five key areas:

### 1. Cash position

Check how much cash and bank balance is available.

Low cash may indicate difficulty in paying daily expenses.

### 2. Receivables

Check how much money customers owe.

High receivables may mean sales are happening, but cash is not coming in.

### 3. Inventory

Check how much money is blocked in stock.

High inventory may mean overstocking or slow-moving goods.

### 4. Payables and creditors

Check how much the business owes to suppliers.

High payables may indicate cash flow pressure.

### 5. Loans

Check total loan outstanding and repayment pressure.

Too much debt can reduce business flexibility.

## Common balance sheet warning signs for MSMEs

A balance sheet may reveal financial problems early.

Warning signs include:

* Cash balance is very low
* Receivables are increasing every month
* Inventory is high but sales are not growing
* Supplier dues are overdue
* Short-term loans are increasing
* Owner is withdrawing too much money
* GST or tax liabilities are unpaid
* Business has high debt compared to equity
* Current liabilities are higher than current assets
* Fixed assets are purchased using short-term borrowing

These signs do not always mean the business is failing, but they need attention.

---

## Difference between balance sheet and profit and loss statement

The balance sheet and profit and loss statement are different but connected.

A profit and loss statement shows income, expenses, and profit over a period.

A balance sheet shows assets, liabilities, and equity on a specific date.

Example:

```text
Profit and Loss Statement:
Shows profit from 1 April 2025 to 31 March 2026.

Balance Sheet:
Shows financial position as on 31 March 2026.
```

Profit affects equity in the balance sheet.

If the business earns profit, equity usually increases.

If the business makes a loss, equity usually decreases.

---

## Difference between profit and cash

Profit and cash are not the same.

A business can be profitable but still have low cash.

Example:

```text
Sales made on credit: ₹5,00,000
Expenses paid in cash: ₹3,00,000
Profit may be visible, but cash may still be low because customers have not paid yet.
```

Profit is based on income and expenses.

Cash is based on actual money received and paid.

The balance sheet helps identify where cash is stuck, such as:

* Receivables
* Inventory
* Advances
* Deposits
* Fixed assets

---

## Balance sheet and cash flow connection

The balance sheet affects cash flow.

Cash flow can improve when:

* Customers pay receivables
* Inventory is sold
* Owner invests more capital
* Loan is received
* Supplier gives longer credit period

Cash flow can reduce when:

* Inventory is purchased
* Supplier payments are made
* Loan EMI is paid
* Tax dues are paid
* Owner withdraws money
* Fixed assets are purchased

A balance sheet helps explain why cash has increased or decreased.

## Important balance sheet ratios

Ratios help analyze the balance sheet more clearly.

### Current ratio

Formula:

```text
Current Ratio = Current Assets / Current Liabilities
```

Example:

```text
Current assets: ₹8,00,000
Current liabilities: ₹4,00,000
Current ratio: 2.0
```

A current ratio above 1 means current assets are higher than current liabilities.

A very low current ratio may indicate short-term payment risk.

---

### Debt to equity ratio

Formula:

```text
Debt to Equity Ratio = Total Debt / Equity
```

Example:

```text
Total debt: ₹6,00,000
Equity: ₹3,00,000
Debt to equity ratio: 2.0
```

This means debt is two times the owner’s equity.

A high debt to equity ratio may mean the business depends heavily on borrowed funds.

---

### Inventory to current assets ratio

Formula:

```text
Inventory to Current Assets Ratio = Inventory / Current Assets
```

Example:

```text
Inventory: ₹4,00,000
Current assets: ₹8,00,000
Ratio: 50%
```

This means half of current assets are stuck in inventory.

For MSMEs, too much inventory can create cash pressure.

---

### Receivables to current assets ratio

Formula:

```text
Receivables to Current Assets Ratio = Receivables / Current Assets
```

Example:

```text
Receivables: ₹3,00,000
Current assets: ₹8,00,000
Ratio: 37.5%
```

This means 37.5% of current assets are money yet to be collected from customers.

High receivables may indicate collection delays.

## Practical MSME example

A small wholesale business has the following balances:

```text
Cash: ₹50,000
Bank: ₹1,00,000
Inventory: ₹5,00,000
Customer receivables: ₹4,00,000
Machinery: ₹3,00,000

Supplier payable: ₹3,50,000
GST payable: ₹50,000
Business loan: ₹4,00,000
Owner capital: ₹5,50,000
```

Balance sheet:

```text
Total assets:
Cash + Bank + Inventory + Receivables + Machinery
₹50,000 + ₹1,00,000 + ₹5,00,000 + ₹4,00,000 + ₹3,00,000
= ₹13,50,000

Total liabilities:
Supplier payable + GST payable + Business loan
₹3,50,000 + ₹50,000 + ₹4,00,000
= ₹8,00,000

Equity:
Owner capital = ₹5,50,000

Assets = Liabilities + Equity
₹13,50,000 = ₹8,00,000 + ₹5,50,000
```

Analysis:

The business has assets worth ₹13,50,000, but only ₹1,50,000 is available as cash and bank balance. A large amount is blocked in inventory and receivables. The business must collect customer payments and manage inventory carefully to avoid cash shortage.

## Common mistakes in MSME balance sheets

Common mistakes include:

* Mixing personal and business expenses
* Not recording customer receivables
* Not recording supplier payables
* Treating loans as income
* Treating owner withdrawals as expenses
* Not updating inventory value
* Ignoring depreciation
* Forgetting GST payable
* Recording advance payments incorrectly
* Not separating short-term and long-term loans
* Not matching bank balance with bank statement

These mistakes can make the business look healthier or weaker than it actually is.

## Balance sheet validation checks

A basic balance sheet should pass these checks:

### Check 1: Assets must equal liabilities plus equity

```text
Total Assets = Total Liabilities + Total Equity
```

If this does not match, the balance sheet has an error.

### Check 2: Current assets should not be negative

Current assets such as cash, receivables, and inventory should normally not be negative.

### Check 3: Liabilities should be recorded clearly

Loans, payables, GST dues, and outstanding expenses should be separated.

### Check 4: Owner withdrawals should not be shown as business expenses

Drawings reduce equity. They are not normal business expenses.

### Check 5: Loan principal and interest should be separated

Loan principal affects the balance sheet.

Loan interest affects the profit and loss statement.

### Check 6: Inventory should be realistic

Inventory value should match actual stock records as closely as possible.

## Simple explanation for non-accounting users

A balance sheet is like a financial photo of a business.

It tells:

```text
What the business owns
What the business owes
What is left for the owner
```

Assets are what the business owns.

Liabilities are what the business owes.

Equity is what belongs to the owner.

Example:

```text
Business owns: ₹10,00,000
Business owes: ₹6,00,000
Owner’s value: ₹4,00,000
```

So the balance sheet shows whether the business is financially strong, weak, risky, or stable.

## Glossary

### Asset

Something owned or controlled by the business that has value.

Example: cash, inventory, machinery.

### Liability

Something the business owes to others.

Example: supplier dues, bank loan, GST payable.

### Equity

Owner’s share in the business after liabilities are deducted from assets.

### Current asset

Asset expected to be converted into cash or used within one year.

Example: cash, receivables, inventory.

### Current liability

Amount payable within one year.

Example: supplier payable, GST payable, salary payable.

### Accounts receivable

Money customers owe to the business.

### Accounts payable

Money the business owes to suppliers.

### Inventory

Goods or materials held for sale, production, or use.

### Fixed asset

Long-term asset used in the business.

Example: machinery, furniture, vehicles.

### Depreciation

Reduction in value of a fixed asset over time.

### Loan

Borrowed money that must be repaid.

### Creditor

A person or business to whom money is owed.

### Working capital

Current assets minus current liabilities.

### Drawings

Money withdrawn by the owner for personal use.

### Retained earnings

Profit kept inside the business instead of being withdrawn.

---

## Summary

A balance sheet is one of the most important financial statements for MSMEs.

It shows:

```text
Assets = Liabilities + Equity
```

Assets show what the business owns.

Liabilities show what the business owes.

Equity shows what belongs to the owner.

For MSMEs, the balance sheet is especially useful for understanding cash flow, receivables, inventory, creditors, loans, and working capital.

A business should regularly review its balance sheet to identify cash shortages, high debt, unpaid customer balances, excess inventory, and financial risk.
