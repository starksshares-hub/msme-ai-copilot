# Budgeting and Forecasting for Small Businesses

## Meaning

Budgeting and forecasting help a business plan its money before problems happen.

A **budget** is a planned estimate of income and expenses for a future period, usually monthly.

A **forecast** is an expected prediction based on past data, current trends, customer demand, season, and business conditions.

Budgeting answers:

> “How much money should we spend?”

Forecasting answers:

> “How much money are we likely to earn or need?”

---

## Why Budgeting and Forecasting Matter

Small businesses need budgeting and forecasting because they help in:

* Controlling expenses
* Planning purchases
* Avoiding cash shortage
* Managing salaries, rent, bills, and supplier payments
* Preparing for slow sales months
* Deciding whether the business can afford expansion
* Understanding future profit or loss
* Reducing financial surprises

A business without budgeting may earn money but still face cash problems.

---

## Monthly Budget

A monthly budget shows expected income and expected expenses for one month.

### Common Income Items

* Sales revenue
* Service income
* Online orders
* Credit customer collections
* Other income

### Common Expense Items

* Rent
* Salary and wages
* Electricity
* Raw materials
* Transport
* Marketing
* Software subscriptions
* Repairs and maintenance
* Loan EMI
* GST or tax payments
* Supplier payments

### Simple Monthly Budget Format

| Item         | Budgeted Amount | Actual Amount | Difference |
| ------------ | --------------: | ------------: | ---------: |
| Sales Income |       ₹5,00,000 |     ₹4,70,000 |   -₹30,000 |
| Rent         |         ₹40,000 |       ₹40,000 |         ₹0 |
| Salary       |       ₹1,20,000 |     ₹1,25,000 |    -₹5,000 |
| Electricity  |         ₹15,000 |       ₹18,000 |    -₹3,000 |
| Marketing    |         ₹25,000 |       ₹20,000 |     ₹5,000 |

---

## Budget Variance

Budget variance means the difference between planned amount and actual amount.

### Formula

```text
Budget Variance = Actual Amount - Budgeted Amount
```

If actual expense is higher than budget, it is usually negative for business.

If actual sales are lower than budget, it is also negative.

### Example

Budgeted electricity expense = ₹15,000
Actual electricity expense = ₹18,000

```text
Variance = ₹18,000 - ₹15,000
Variance = ₹3,000 extra expense
```

This means the business spent ₹3,000 more than planned.

## Sales Forecast

A sales forecast predicts expected sales for a future period.

Sales forecast can be based on:

* Previous month sales
* Same month last year sales
* Seasonal demand
* Customer orders
* Market conditions
* Festivals or holidays
* Marketing campaigns
* New product launches

### Example

If a shop sold ₹4,00,000 last month and expects 10% growth next month:

```text
Forecasted Sales = ₹4,00,000 + 10%
Forecasted Sales = ₹4,40,000
```

---

## Expense Forecast

An expense forecast predicts expected future expenses.

Expenses can be divided into:

### Fixed Expenses

These remain mostly the same every month.

Examples:

* Rent
* Salaries
* Insurance
* Software subscriptions
* Loan EMI

### Variable Expenses

These change with sales or production.

Examples:

* Raw materials
* Packaging
* Delivery charges
* Electricity
* Sales commission
* Marketing

If sales increase, variable expenses usually increase too.

---

## Cash Forecast

A cash forecast predicts how much cash the business will have in future.

It focuses on cash coming in and cash going out.

Profit and cash are not always the same.

A business can show profit but still have cash shortage if customers do not pay on time.

### Cash Inflows

* Cash sales
* Customer payments
* Loan received
* Owner capital
* Advance received

### Cash Outflows

* Supplier payments
* Rent
* Salary
* Electricity
* Loan EMI
* Tax payments
* Inventory purchase
* Repairs

### Basic Cash Forecast Formula

```text
Closing Cash = Opening Cash + Cash Inflows - Cash Outflows
```

### Example

Opening cash = ₹80,000
Cash inflow = ₹3,00,000
Cash outflow = ₹2,60,000

```text
Closing Cash = ₹80,000 + ₹3,00,000 - ₹2,60,000
Closing Cash = ₹1,20,000
```

The business is expected to have ₹1,20,000 cash at month end.

---

## Difference Between Budget and Forecast

| Budget                                   | Forecast                       |
| ---------------------------------------- | ------------------------------ |
| Planned target                           | Expected result                |
| Usually fixed for a period               | Updated regularly              |
| Shows what the business wants to achieve | Shows what is likely to happen |
| Used for control                         | Used for prediction            |
| Based on goals                           | Based on data and trends       |

---

## Example: Simple Business Forecast

A small business has the following expected figures for next month:

| Particulars               |    Amount |
| ------------------------- | --------: |
| Opening Cash              |   ₹50,000 |
| Expected Sales Collection | ₹3,00,000 |
| Supplier Payment          | ₹1,20,000 |
| Rent                      |   ₹30,000 |
| Salary                    |   ₹80,000 |
| Electricity               |   ₹10,000 |
| Loan EMI                  |   ₹20,000 |

Total Cash Inflow:

```text
₹3,00,000
```

Total Cash Outflow:

```text
₹1,20,000 + ₹30,000 + ₹80,000 + ₹10,000 + ₹20,000 = ₹2,60,000
```

Closing Cash:

```text
₹50,000 + ₹3,00,000 - ₹2,60,000 = ₹90,000
```

The business is expected to have ₹90,000 cash left.

## Warning Signs in Budgeting and Forecasting

A business should be careful if:

* Actual expenses are always higher than budget
* Sales forecast is too optimistic
* Cash balance keeps decreasing
* Customer payments are delayed
* Inventory purchase is more than sales demand
* Loan EMI is too high compared to cash inflow
* Business depends on one or two large customers
* No emergency cash buffer is maintained

---

## How Budgeting Helps Business Decisions

Budgeting helps answer questions like:

* Can we afford to hire one more employee?
* Can we purchase more inventory this month?
* Do we have enough cash for rent and salary?
* Should we reduce marketing expenses?
* Are supplier payments becoming difficult?
* Will we need a business loan?
* Which expenses are increasing too much?

---

## Good Budgeting Practices

A small business should:

* Prepare budget every month
* Compare budget with actual results
* Track all expenses properly
* Keep emergency cash reserve
* Update forecast when sales change
* Separate personal and business expenses
* Avoid overestimating future sales
* Plan for tax and EMI payments
* Review overdue customer payments
* Control unnecessary spending

---

## Common Mistakes

Common budgeting and forecasting mistakes include:

* Ignoring small daily expenses
* Not tracking cash sales properly
* Assuming all credit sales will be collected quickly
* Forgetting GST, tax, EMI, and annual expenses
* Not planning for slow months
* Buying too much inventory
* Mixing business and personal money
* Not updating forecasts regularly
