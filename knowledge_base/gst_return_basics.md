# Bank Reconciliation for MSMEs

## 1. What is Bank Reconciliation?

Bank reconciliation means matching the transactions recorded in a business’s books with the transactions shown in the bank statement.

It helps a business confirm whether the cash or bank balance shown in its accounting records is correct.

In simple words:

> Bank reconciliation checks whether the money recorded by the business matches the money actually shown by the bank.

For MSMEs, bank reconciliation is important because many transactions happen daily through UPI, NEFT, RTGS, IMPS, cheques, cash deposits, card payments, loan EMIs, vendor payments, customer receipts, bank charges, and GST-related payments.

---

## 2. Why Bank Reconciliation is Important

Bank reconciliation helps MSMEs detect:

* Missing transactions
* Duplicate entries
* Wrong payment entries
* Unrecorded bank charges
* Customer payments not entered in books
* Vendor payments recorded but not cleared by bank
* Cheques issued but not presented
* Cheques deposited but not cleared
* Wrong UPI or bank transfer entries
* Fraudulent or suspicious transactions
* Incorrect opening or closing bank balance
* Accounting mistakes

Without bank reconciliation, a business may think it has more money than it actually has.

This can cause poor cash flow decisions, missed vendor payments, overdraft problems, and wrong financial reports.

---

## 3. Main Purpose of Bank Reconciliation

The main purpose is to explain the difference between:

1. Balance as per bank statement
2. Balance as per business books or cash book

The difference may happen because some transactions are recorded in one place but not the other.

For example, the business may record a vendor payment today, but the bank may process it tomorrow. Until the bank processes it, the two balances will not match.

---

## 4. Common Reasons Why Bank Balance Does Not Match Books

### 4.1 Bank Charges Not Recorded

Banks may deduct charges such as:

* SMS charges
* Debit card charges
* Cheque book charges
* NEFT/RTGS charges
* IMPS charges
* Account maintenance fees
* Payment gateway charges
* POS machine charges

These charges appear in the bank statement, but the business may forget to record them in its books.

Example:

Bank statement shows:

```text
Bank Charges: ₹250
```

But the accounting records do not show this expense.

This creates a difference of ₹250.

---

### 4.2 Interest Credited by Bank Not Recorded

Sometimes the bank credits interest on savings accounts, current account balances, fixed deposits, or other bank products.

Example:

Bank statement shows:

```text
Interest Credit: ₹1,200
```

But the business has not recorded it as income.

This causes the bank balance to be higher than the book balance.

---

### 4.3 Cheque Issued But Not Presented

A business may issue a cheque to a supplier, but the supplier may not deposit it immediately.

The business records the payment, but the bank does not deduct the amount until the cheque is cleared.

Example:

Business records:

```text
Payment to Supplier A: ₹25,000
```

Bank statement does not show the payment yet.

This means the book balance is lower than the bank balance.

---

### 4.4 Cheque Deposited But Not Cleared

A customer may give a cheque to the business. The business deposits it and records it as received, but the bank may take time to clear it.

Example:

Business records:

```text
Received from Customer B: ₹40,000
```

Bank statement does not show the credit yet.

This means the book balance is higher than the bank balance.

---

### 4.5 Customer Payment Received But Not Recorded

Customers may pay through:

* UPI
* NEFT
* RTGS
* IMPS
* QR code
* Payment gateway
* Cash deposit
* Bank transfer

Sometimes the money is credited to the bank account, but the business forgets to record it in books.

Example:

Bank statement shows:

```text
UPI Credit from Customer: ₹7,500
```

But the accounting records do not contain this receipt.

This causes mismatch.

---

### 4.6 Vendor Payment Recorded Twice

A business may accidentally record the same supplier payment twice.

Example:

Books show:

```text
Payment to Vendor X: ₹15,000
Payment to Vendor X: ₹15,000
```

But bank statement shows only one payment of ₹15,000.

This means the books are showing less bank balance than actual.

---

### 4.7 Duplicate Customer Receipt Entry

A customer receipt may be entered twice in the books.

Example:

Books show:

```text
Received from Customer Y: ₹10,000
Received from Customer Y: ₹10,000
```

Bank statement shows only one credit of ₹10,000.

This means the books are showing extra income and extra bank balance.

---

### 4.8 Wrong Amount Entered

Sometimes the transaction exists in both bank statement and books, but the amount is different.

Example:

Bank statement shows:

```text
Payment to Supplier: ₹18,500
```

Books show:

```text
Payment to Supplier: ₹15,800
```

Difference:

```text
₹18,500 - ₹15,800 = ₹2,700
```

This may happen due to typing error or wrong invoice matching.

---

### 4.9 Wrong Date Entered

A transaction may be recorded on the wrong date.

Example:

Bank statement date:

```text
5 April 2026
```

Books date:

```text
15 April 2026
```

This may create mismatch during monthly reconciliation.

The transaction exists, but it may appear in a different accounting period.

---

### 4.10 Loan EMI Deducted But Not Recorded

Banks may auto-debit loan EMIs.

Example:

Bank statement shows:

```text
Loan EMI Debit: ₹22,000
```

But the business has not recorded:

* Loan principal repayment
* Interest expense
* Bank debit

This creates mismatch and also affects the balance sheet and profit and loss statement.

---

### 4.11 GST Payment Not Recorded

A business may pay GST through the bank, but forget to record it properly in the books.

Example:

Bank statement shows:

```text
GST Payment: ₹18,000
```

Books do not show GST paid.

This affects both bank reconciliation and GST liability tracking.

---

### 4.12 Cash Deposit Not Recorded

The business may deposit cash into the bank account but forget to record it.

Example:

Bank statement shows:

```text
Cash Deposit: ₹50,000
```

Books do not show the source of this cash.

This may create confusion during accounting or tax review.

---

### 4.13 Payment Gateway Settlement Difference

Businesses using Razorpay, PhonePe, Paytm, Stripe, Cashfree, or other payment gateways may receive settlement amounts after deductions.

Example:

Customer paid:

```text
₹10,000
```

Payment gateway deducted charges:

```text
₹200
```

Bank received:

```text
₹9,800
```

If books record ₹10,000 as bank receipt, but the bank statement shows ₹9,800, there will be a mismatch.

Correct treatment:

```text
Bank Receipt: ₹9,800
Payment Gateway Charges: ₹200
Total Customer Payment: ₹10,000
```

## 5. Important Terms in Bank Reconciliation

### Bank Statement

A bank statement is a record provided by the bank showing all deposits, withdrawals, charges, interest, and closing balance for a specific period.

### Cash Book

A cash book or bank book is the business’s internal record of cash and bank transactions.

### Book Balance

Book balance means the bank balance according to the business’s accounting records.

### Bank Balance

Bank balance means the balance shown in the bank statement.

### Outstanding Cheque

An outstanding cheque is a cheque issued by the business but not yet cleared by the bank.

### Deposit in Transit

A deposit in transit is money deposited or recorded by the business but not yet credited by the bank.

### Bank Charges

Bank charges are fees deducted by the bank for services.

### Unpresented Cheque

An unpresented cheque is a cheque given to someone but not yet submitted to the bank for payment.

### Uncredited Deposit

An uncredited deposit is a deposit recorded in books but not yet shown in the bank statement.

## 6. Bank Reconciliation Formula

A common reconciliation formula is:

```text
Balance as per Cash Book
Add: Receipts shown in bank but not in books
Add: Cheques issued but not cleared
Less: Payments shown in bank but not in books
Less: Cheques deposited but not cleared
= Balance as per Bank Statement
```

Another way:

```text
Balance as per Bank Statement
Add: Deposits recorded in books but not credited by bank
Add: Bank debits not recorded in books
Less: Cheques issued but not presented
Less: Bank credits not recorded in books
= Balance as per Books
```

The exact formula depends on whether reconciliation starts from bank statement balance or book balance.

## 7. Simple Bank Reconciliation Example

### Situation

Book balance as per business records:

```text
₹1,00,000
```

Bank statement balance:

```text
₹1,12,500
```

Differences found:

```text
Bank charges not recorded: ₹500
Customer payment received in bank but not recorded: ₹20,000
Cheque issued to supplier but not cleared: ₹8,000
Cheque deposited but not cleared: ₹15,000
```

### Reconciliation

Start with book balance:

```text
Book Balance: ₹1,00,000
Less: Bank charges not recorded: ₹500
Add: Customer payment not recorded: ₹20,000
Add: Cheque issued but not cleared: ₹8,000
Less: Cheque deposited but not cleared: ₹15,000
```

Calculation:

```text
₹1,00,000 - ₹500 + ₹20,000 + ₹8,000 - ₹15,000 = ₹1,12,500
```

Reconciled bank balance:

```text
₹1,12,500
```

This matches the bank statement.

## 10. Common Bank Reconciliation Problems for MSMEs

### Problem 1: Bank statement has many UPI transactions

Small businesses may receive many UPI payments every day. If customer names are not clear, it becomes hard to match receipts with invoices.

Example:

```text
UPI/CR/AMIT/7823
UPI/CR/RAJ/9231
UPI/CR/QRSCAN/5532
```

The business may not know which invoice these payments belong to.

Possible solution:

* Record customer name at time of payment
* Mention invoice number in payment note
* Use QR code linked to billing software
* Reconcile daily instead of monthly

---

### Problem 2: Payment gateway settlements do not match sales

Payment gateway settlement amount may be lower than sales amount due to charges.

Example:

```text
Sales as per invoice: ₹50,000
Payment gateway charges: ₹1,000
Bank settlement: ₹49,000
```

If the business records only ₹49,000 as sales, revenue becomes understated.

If the business records ₹50,000 as bank receipt, bank balance becomes overstated.

Correct entry:

```text
Sales / Customer Receipt: ₹50,000
Bank Receipt: ₹49,000
Gateway Charges Expense: ₹1,000
```

---

### Problem 3: Cash and bank records are mixed

Some MSMEs do not separate cash and bank transactions.

Example:

A customer pays cash, but the business records it as bank receipt.

This causes mismatch because the bank statement will not show that receipt.

---

### Problem 4: Owner personal expenses paid from business bank

Sometimes the owner pays personal expenses from the business bank account.

Examples:

* Personal shopping
* Family expenses
* Personal travel
* Personal loan EMI

These transactions appear in bank statement but may not be recorded properly in books.

They should usually be recorded as drawings or owner withdrawal, not business expense.

---

### Problem 5: Vendor paid but invoice missing

A bank statement may show payment to a vendor, but there may be no invoice in records.

This creates problems because:

* Expense cannot be verified
* GST input tax credit may be affected
* Payment may be duplicate or suspicious
* Profit and loss report may be inaccurate

---

## 11. How to Do Bank Reconciliation Step-by-Step

### Step 1: Collect Records

Collect:

* Bank statement
* Cash book
* Bank book
* Sales invoices
* Purchase invoices
* Payment receipts
* Cheque register
* Loan statements
* GST payment challans
* Payment gateway reports

---

### Step 2: Match Opening Balance

Check whether the opening balance in books matches the previous period’s closing reconciled balance.

If opening balance is wrong, the entire reconciliation may be wrong.

---

### Step 3: Match Deposits and Credits

Compare all credits in the bank statement with receipts recorded in books.

Bank credits may include:

* Customer payments
* Cash deposits
* Loan received
* Interest income
* Refunds
* Capital introduced
* Payment gateway settlements

---

### Step 4: Match Withdrawals and Debits

Compare all debits in the bank statement with payments recorded in books.

Bank debits may include:

* Vendor payments
* Salary payments
* Rent payments
* Electricity bills
* Loan EMIs
* GST payments
* TDS payments
* Bank charges
* Card charges
* Cash withdrawals

---

### Step 5: Identify Unmatched Bank Transactions

Find transactions that appear in the bank statement but not in books.

Examples:

```text
Bank charges
Interest credited
Auto-debit EMI
Customer payment not recorded
Refund received
Unknown debit
```

---

### Step 6: Identify Unmatched Book Transactions

Find transactions that appear in books but not in bank statement.

Examples:

```text
Cheque issued but not cleared
Cheque deposited but not cleared
Wrong bank account selected
Duplicate entry
Future-dated transaction
```

---

### Step 7: Record Missing Entries

After identifying missing transactions, pass proper accounting entries.

Examples:

Bank charges:

```text
Bank Charges Expense Dr.
    To Bank Account
```

Interest credited:

```text
Bank Account Dr.
    To Interest Income
```

Loan EMI:

```text
Loan Account Dr.
Interest Expense Dr.
    To Bank Account
```

---

### Step 8: Adjust Errors

Correct:

* Wrong amounts
* Wrong dates
* Wrong customer names
* Wrong vendor names
* Duplicate entries
* Incorrect bank account selection
* Incorrect expense category

---

### Step 9: Prepare Reconciliation Statement

Prepare a summary showing:

* Balance as per books
* Additions
* Deductions
* Adjusted balance
* Balance as per bank statement
* Difference, if any

---

### Step 10: Confirm Final Difference is Zero

After reconciliation, the difference should ideally be zero.

If the difference is not zero, there are still unmatched or incorrect transactions.

## 13. Red Flags in Bank Reconciliation

The following signs may indicate mistakes or fraud:

* Large unexplained withdrawals
* Duplicate vendor payments
* Payments to unknown parties
* Customer payments not linked to invoices
* Bank debits without supporting bills
* Frequent cash withdrawals
* Round-number payments without explanation
* Same amount paid multiple times
* Bank balance not reconciled for many months
* Owner personal expenses mixed with business expenses
* Missing bank charges and EMI entries
* Difference carried forward every month without solving

## 15. Bank Reconciliation and Cash Flow

Bank reconciliation directly affects cash flow planning.

If bank records are not accurate, the business may make wrong decisions.

Example:

Books show bank balance:

```text
₹2,00,000
```

But actual bank balance is:

```text
₹1,20,000
```

The owner may wrongly assume there is enough money to pay suppliers, salaries, or rent.

This can create cash shortage.

Bank reconciliation helps match customer payments with unpaid invoices.

Example:

Customer invoice:

```text

Amount: ₹25,000
Status: Unpaid
```

Bank statement:

```text
NEFT Credit from Customer: ₹25,000
```

If this payment is not matched, the invoice may still show as unpaid even though the customer has paid.

This creates wrong accounts receivable reports.

## 22. Useful Matching Rules

A reconciliation system can match transactions using:

### Exact Amount Match

If invoice amount and bank credit amount are the same, they may be matched.

Example:

```text
Invoice amount: ₹12,000
Bank credit: ₹12,000
```

### Date Range Match

A payment may be matched if it happened within a few days of invoice date or due date.

Example:

```text
Invoice date: 05 April
Payment received: 07 April
```

### Reference Number Match

Bank reference numbers, UTR numbers, or UPI transaction IDs can help match payments.

### Customer Name Match

Customer name in bank narration can be matched with invoice customer name.

### Vendor Name Match

Vendor name in bank narration can be matched with purchase records.

### Partial Payment Match

Sometimes customers pay part of an invoice.

Example:

```text
Invoice amount: ₹50,000
Bank receipt: ₹20,000
Remaining due: ₹30,000
```

### Multiple Invoice Match

One bank payment may settle multiple invoices.

Example:

```text
Bank receipt: ₹75,000

Invoice 1: ₹25,000
Invoice 2: ₹30,000
Invoice 3: ₹20,000

Total: ₹75,000
```

### Net Settlement Match

Payment gateway settlement may be matched after deducting fees.

Example:

```text
Gross customer payment: ₹10,000
Gateway fee: ₹200
Net bank receipt: ₹9,800
```

## 24. Example AI/RAG Questions and Answers

### Question

Why is my bank balance different from my books?

### Answer

Your bank balance may be different from your books because some transactions are recorded in the bank statement but not in your accounting records, or recorded in your accounting records but not yet cleared by the bank. Common reasons include bank charges, interest credits, uncleared cheques, customer payments not recorded, duplicate entries, loan EMIs, payment gateway deductions, or wrong amount entries.

---

### Question

What are uncleared cheques?

### Answer

Uncleared cheques are cheques issued or deposited by the business but not yet processed by the bank. If a cheque is issued to a supplier but not deposited by the supplier, the business records the payment, but the bank balance does not reduce yet.

---

### Question

What should I check first in bank reconciliation?

### Answer

First check whether the opening balance in the books matches the previous reconciled closing balance. Then compare all bank credits with receipts in the books and all bank debits with payments in the books.

---

### Question

How do bank charges affect reconciliation?

### Answer

Bank charges are deducted directly by the bank. If they are not recorded in the business books, the bank statement balance will be lower than the book balance. The business should record bank charges as an expense.

---

### Question

Why does payment gateway settlement not match sales?

### Answer

Payment gateway settlement may be lower than sales because the payment gateway deducts charges before transferring money to the bank. The business should record the full sale amount, the gateway charge as an expense, and the net amount received in the bank.

## 25. Practical Example for MSME

A small business has the following records:

### Books

```text
Opening bank balance: ₹50,000
Customer receipt: ₹30,000
Supplier payment: ₹15,000
Rent payment: ₹10,000
Closing balance as per books: ₹55,000
```

### Bank Statement

```text
Opening bank balance: ₹50,000
Customer receipt: ₹30,000
Supplier payment: ₹15,000
Rent payment: ₹10,000
Bank charges: ₹300
Interest credited: ₹500
Closing balance as per bank: ₹55,200
```

### Difference

Books show:

```text
₹55,000
```

Bank shows:

```text
₹55,200
```

Reason:

```text
Bank charges not recorded: ₹300
Interest credited not recorded: ₹500
```

Adjustment:

```text
Book balance: ₹55,000
Less bank charges: ₹300
Add interest income: ₹500
Adjusted balance: ₹55,200
```

Now the books match the bank statement.

## 26. Best Practices for MSMEs

MSMEs should:

* Reconcile bank accounts weekly or monthly
* Use separate business and personal bank accounts
* Record every bank transaction
* Download bank statements regularly
* Match customer payments with invoices
* Record bank charges immediately
* Track cheques issued and deposited
* Review payment gateway settlement reports
* Avoid duplicate entries
* Investigate unknown transactions quickly
* Keep invoices and payment proofs
* Use accounting software or spreadsheets
* Review bank reconciliation before preparing financial reports

---

## 27. Simple Summary

Bank reconciliation is the process of matching the business’s bank statement with its accounting records.

It helps find missing transactions, bank charges, wrong entries, duplicate payments, uncleared cheques, and suspicious transactions.

For MSMEs, bank reconciliation is important because it keeps cash flow, invoices, vendor payments, GST records, profit and loss, and balance sheet reports accurate.

A properly reconciled bank account means the business owner can trust the bank balance shown in the books.
