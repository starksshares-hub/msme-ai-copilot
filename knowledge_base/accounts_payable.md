# Accounts Payable

## Meaning

Accounts payable means the money a business owes to suppliers, vendors, service providers, or creditors.

It usually comes from goods or services purchased on credit.

For an MSME, accounts payable may include unpaid supplier bills, raw material purchases, inventory purchases, rent payable, electricity bills payable, transport bills payable, GST payable, and other pending business expenses.

---

## Simple Explanation

Accounts payable is money the business has to pay in the future.

If a business buys goods today but pays after 15 days, 30 days, or 60 days, that unpaid amount becomes accounts payable.

Example:

A business buys inventory worth ₹80,000 from a supplier on 30-day credit.

The business has received the goods, but payment has not yet been made.

This ₹80,000 is accounts payable.

---

## Why Accounts Payable Matters

Accounts payable is important because it affects cash flow.

If many bills are due at the same time, the business may face cash pressure.

If the business delays supplier payments too much, suppliers may stop giving credit or may refuse future orders.

Good accounts payable management helps the business:

* Pay suppliers on time.
* Avoid late payment penalties.
* Maintain good vendor relationships.
* Plan cash outflows properly.
* Avoid sudden cash shortages.
* Use vendor credit wisely.
* Understand upcoming liabilities.

## Accounts Payable Example

A business has the following unpaid bills:

| Vendor / Payee     | Bill Type          |  Amount | Due Date    | Status  |
| ------------------ | ------------------ | ------: | ----------- | ------- |
| Sharma Traders     | Inventory purchase | ₹75,000 | 10 May 2024 | Unpaid  |
| FastMove Logistics | Transport charges  | ₹12,000 | 12 May 2024 | Unpaid  |
| City Power Board   | Electricity bill   |  ₹8,500 | 15 May 2024 | Unpaid  |
| Office Owner       | Shop rent          | ₹35,000 | 5 May 2024  | Overdue |
| Mehta Packaging    | Packaging material | ₹18,000 | 20 May 2024 | Unpaid  |

**Total Accounts Payable:** ₹1,48,500

This means the business must pay ₹1,48,500 to suppliers and service providers.

---

## Accounts Payable Formula

**Accounts Payable = Total Unpaid Supplier Bills + Total Unpaid Business Expenses**

Example:

**Accounts Payable = ₹75,000 + ₹12,000 + ₹8,500 + ₹35,000 + ₹18,000**

**Accounts Payable = ₹1,48,500**

## Payment Priority

Not all payables have the same urgency.

A business should prioritize payments based on importance and risk.

High-priority payments may include:

* Overdue supplier bills.
* Rent payable.
* GST or tax payable.
* Salary payable.
* Bills from critical suppliers.
* Bills with late payment penalties.
* Vendors who may stop supply if unpaid.

Lower-priority payments may include:

* Bills with longer credit period.
* Non-urgent service bills.
* Vendors who allow flexible payment terms.
* Payments that do not immediately affect operations.

---

## Vendor Credit

Vendor credit means suppliers allow the business to buy now and pay later.

Common credit periods include:

* 7 days
* 15 days
* 30 days
* 45 days
* 60 days

Vendor credit helps businesses manage working capital because they can sell goods before paying suppliers.

However, overusing vendor credit can be risky if the business cannot pay on time.

---

## Good Vendor Credit Example

A retailer buys goods worth ₹1,00,000 on 30-day credit.

The retailer sells the goods within 20 days and collects money from customers.

The retailer then pays the supplier on day 30.

This is good use of vendor credit because supplier credit helped the business operate smoothly.

---

## Bad Vendor Credit Example

A retailer buys goods worth ₹1,00,000 on 30-day credit.

The goods do not sell quickly.

The supplier payment becomes due, but the business does not have enough cash.

The business delays payment and damages the supplier relationship.

This is poor vendor credit management.

---

## Accounts Payable and Cash Flow

Accounts payable directly affects cash flow.

When the business pays suppliers, cash goes out.

If large payments are due together, the business may face cash shortage.

Example:

| Upcoming Payment |  Amount |
| ---------------- | ------: |
| Supplier payment | ₹90,000 |
| Rent             | ₹35,000 |
| GST payment      | ₹28,000 |
| Salary payable   | ₹75,000 |

**Total Upcoming Cash Outflow:** ₹2,28,000

If the business has only ₹1,20,000 cash available, it has a cash gap of ₹1,08,000.

---

## Cash Gap Formula

**Cash Gap = Total Due Payments - Available Cash**

Example:

**Cash Gap = ₹2,28,000 - ₹1,20,000**

**Cash Gap = ₹1,08,000**

The business needs ₹1,08,000 more to meet upcoming payments.

---

## Accounts Payable Warning Signs

The following signs show accounts payable risk:

* Supplier bills are frequently overdue.
* Business pays vendors only after repeated reminders.
* GST or tax payments are delayed.
* Rent or salary payments are pending.
* Vendor credit period is reducing.
* Suppliers refuse to provide goods on credit.
* Business uses loans to pay old supplier bills.
* Total payables are increasing faster than sales.
* Payables are high but cash balance is low.
* Many bills are older than 60 or 90 days.

---

## Common MSME Accounts Payable Problems

MSMEs often face accounts payable problems because of:

* Delayed customer payments.
* Poor cash flow planning.
* No proper bill tracking.
* Mixing personal and business expenses.
* Buying excess inventory on credit.
* Not checking due dates regularly.
* Not maintaining emergency cash reserves.
* Depending too much on vendor credit.
* Not negotiating payment terms.

---

## How to Improve Accounts Payable Management

A business can improve accounts payable by:

1. Recording every supplier bill immediately.
2. Tracking due dates clearly.
3. Preparing weekly payment plans.
4. Prioritizing urgent and critical payments.
5. Negotiating longer credit periods with suppliers.
6. Avoiding unnecessary purchases on credit.
7. Matching purchase orders, invoices, and goods received.
8. Paying GST, salary, rent, and critical vendors first.
9. Maintaining a cash reserve for upcoming bills.
10. Reviewing accounts payable aging regularly.

## Accounts Payable vs Accounts Receivable

| Accounts Payable              | Accounts Receivable               |
| ----------------------------- | --------------------------------- |
| Money the business has to pay | Money the business has to collect |
| Liability for the business    | Asset for the business            |
| Owed to suppliers or vendors  | Owed by customers                 |
| Creates future cash outflow   | Creates future cash inflow        |
| Example: unpaid supplier bill | Example: unpaid customer invoice  |

## Simple Example for MSME Owner

A small shop has ₹1,00,000 cash available.

It owes:

* ₹60,000 to suppliers
* ₹25,000 rent
* ₹15,000 electricity and internet bills
* ₹30,000 GST payment

Total payable is:

**₹60,000 + ₹25,000 + ₹15,000 + ₹30,000 = ₹1,30,000**

The shop has only ₹1,00,000 cash but needs ₹1,30,000.

So, the cash gap is:

**₹1,30,000 - ₹1,00,000 = ₹30,000**

This means the shop must arrange ₹30,000 more, collect customer payments faster, delay non-critical payments, or negotiate more time with suppliers.

---

## Final Conclusion

Accounts payable shows how much money the business owes to suppliers, vendors, and service providers.

For MSMEs, accounts payable must be tracked carefully because unpaid bills directly affect cash flow, supplier trust, working capital, and business stability.

Good accounts payable management helps the business avoid penalties, protect vendor relationships, plan payments properly, and prevent sudden cash shortages.
