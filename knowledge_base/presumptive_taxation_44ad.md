# Presumptive Taxation Under Section 44AD

## Short Description

Section 44AD is a simplified income tax scheme for eligible small businesses. Instead of calculating exact profit by maintaining detailed books of accounts, the taxpayer can declare business income as a fixed percentage of turnover or gross receipts.

For Section 44AD, the Income Tax Department says the turnover limit is generally **₹2 crore**, but it can be **₹3 crore** if cash receipts do not exceed 5% of total gross receipts.

---

## What Is Section 44AD?

Section 44AD is a presumptive taxation scheme under the Income-tax Act, 1961. It allows eligible small businesses to calculate taxable income on a presumptive basis.

In simple terms:

```text
Normal business taxation:
Profit = Sales - Expenses
```

```text
Presumptive taxation under Section 44AD:
Taxable business income = Prescribed percentage of turnover
```

This is useful for small businesses that want simpler tax compliance.

---

## Why Section 44AD Exists

Section 44AD is meant to reduce the compliance burden for small taxpayers.

Without presumptive taxation, a business may need to:

* Maintain detailed books of accounts
* Track every expense
* Prepare detailed profit and loss statements
* Possibly get accounts audited

Under Section 44AD, eligible taxpayers can declare income at the prescribed percentage and avoid detailed bookkeeping for that eligible business. The Income Tax Department states that a person adopting Section 44AD and declaring income at 6% or 8%, as applicable, is not required to maintain books of account under Section 44AA for that business.

---

## Who Can Use Section 44AD?

Section 44AD can be used by eligible small business taxpayers.

Eligible taxpayers generally include:

* Resident individuals
* Resident Hindu Undivided Families, also called HUFs
* Resident partnership firms, except LLPs

Example:

```text
A resident individual running a small grocery shop may use Section 44AD if turnover is within the allowed limit.
```

```text
A resident partnership firm running a small trading business may use Section 44AD if it is not an LLP and satisfies the turnover conditions.
```

---

## Turnover Limit Under Section 44AD

The turnover or gross receipts limit depends on the cash receipt percentage.

```text
If cash receipts are more than 5%:
Maximum turnover limit = ₹2 crore
```

```text
If cash receipts are within 5%:
Maximum turnover limit = ₹3 crore
```

The Income Tax Department states that the Section 44AD threshold is ₹3 crore if cash and certain non-account-payee modes do not exceed 5% of total gross receipts, and ₹2 crore otherwise.

---

## Presumptive Income Rate

Under Section 44AD, business income is usually calculated at:

```text
6% of turnover received through banking / digital modes
8% of turnover received in cash or other non-digital modes
```

The Income Tax Department explains that the 6% rate applies instead of 8% where turnover or gross receipts are received through account payee cheque, account payee bank draft, electronic clearing system through a bank account, or prescribed electronic modes.

---

## Simple Formula

```text
Presumptive income =
6% of eligible digital / banking receipts
+
8% of cash / non-digital receipts
```

Example:

```text
Digital receipts = ₹20,00,000
Cash receipts = ₹5,00,000
```

Calculation:

```text
6% of ₹20,00,000 = ₹1,20,000
8% of ₹5,00,000 = ₹40,000
Total presumptive income = ₹1,60,000
```

So, the business may declare ₹1,60,000 as presumptive business income if eligible.

---

## Example: Small Retail Shop

### Case

Ravi runs a small mobile accessories shop.

```text
Digital sales = ₹30,00,000
Cash sales = ₹10,00,000
Total turnover = ₹40,00,000
```

### Calculation

```text
6% of ₹30,00,000 = ₹1,80,000
8% of ₹10,00,000 = ₹80,000
Total presumptive income = ₹2,60,000
```

### Interpretation

Ravi may report ₹2,60,000 as business income under Section 44AD if he satisfies all eligibility conditions.

---

## Example: Mostly Digital Business

### Case

A small trader has total receipts of ₹2.5 crore.

```text
Total receipts = ₹2,50,00,000
Cash receipts = ₹8,00,000
```

Cash percentage:

```text
₹8,00,000 / ₹2,50,00,000 × 100 = 3.2%
```

Since cash receipts are within 5%, the ₹3 crore threshold may apply.

### Interpretation

This business may be within the Section 44AD turnover limit because cash receipts are not more than 5% of total gross receipts.

---

## Example: Cash Receipts Too High

### Case

A trader has total receipts of ₹2.5 crore.

```text
Total receipts = ₹2,50,00,000
Cash receipts = ₹30,00,000
```

Cash percentage:

```text
₹30,00,000 / ₹2,50,00,000 × 100 = 12%
```

Since cash receipts are more than 5%, the ₹3 crore limit does not apply. The regular ₹2 crore limit applies.

### Interpretation

This business may not be eligible for Section 44AD because its turnover is above ₹2 crore and cash receipts are above the 5% condition.

---

## Businesses Not Eligible Under Section 44AD

Section 44AD is not available for every type of business.

The Income Tax Department says Section 44AD is not available for:

* Business of plying, hiring, or leasing goods carriages covered under Section 44AE
* Agency business
* Commission or brokerage income, such as insurance commission
* Businesses exceeding the prescribed turnover limits
* Persons required to maintain books under Section 44AA(1)

Example:

```text
An insurance agent earning commission income cannot use Section 44AD for that commission income.
```

```text
A transport operator covered under Section 44AE should not use Section 44AD for that goods carriage business.
```

---

## Expenses Under Section 44AD

When a taxpayer uses Section 44AD, the presumptive income is treated as final business income.

This means the taxpayer cannot separately deduct normal business expenses like:

* Rent
* Salary
* Electricity
* Transport
* Repairs
* Depreciation
* Office expenses
* Internet expense
* Marketing expense

The Income Tax Department states that after declaring profit at the specified presumptive rate, further deduction of expenses is not allowed, though Chapter VI-A deductions may still be claimed separately if eligible.

---

## Depreciation Treatment

Under Section 44AD, separate depreciation deduction is not available.

However, the written down value of business assets is calculated as if depreciation was already allowed. This means depreciation is considered internally for asset value calculation, but it is not separately deducted again from presumptive income.

Simple meaning:

```text
You cannot reduce presumptive income again by claiming depreciation separately.
```

---

## Books of Accounts

A taxpayer who properly uses Section 44AD and declares income at the applicable presumptive rate does not need to maintain books of account under Section 44AA for that eligible business.

However, practical records should still be kept.

Useful records include:

* Sales summary
* Cash receipt summary
* Digital payment summary
* Bank statements
* GST returns, if registered
* Purchase records
* Basic expense records
* Customer invoices
* Vendor bills
* UPI / POS / payment gateway reports

These records help prove turnover and explain cash vs digital receipts.

---

## Advance Tax Under Section 44AD

A person opting for Section 44AD must pay the whole amount of advance tax on or before **15 March** of the previous year. If they fail to pay advance tax by 15 March, interest under Section 234C may apply.

Simple meaning:

```text
Section 44AD taxpayer should not ignore advance tax.
```

---

## Tax Audit Under Section 44AD

If a taxpayer uses Section 44AD and declares income at the applicable presumptive rate, tax audit under Section 44AB is generally not required for that eligible business.

However, if the taxpayer declares income lower than the presumptive rate and their total income exceeds the basic exemption limit, they may need to maintain books and get accounts audited. Section 44AD itself states that lower declared profits can trigger books and audit requirements when total income exceeds the maximum amount not chargeable to tax.

---

## Five-Year Rule

If a taxpayer opts for Section 44AD, they are generally expected to continue using the scheme for the next five years.

The Income Tax Department explains that if a person opts for presumptive taxation and later does not follow the scheme, the scheme will not be available for the next five assessment years. The taxpayer may also need to maintain books and face tax audit requirements if total income exceeds the basic exemption limit.

Simple example:

```text
A taxpayer uses Section 44AD in one year.
Next year, they opt out without continuing the scheme.
They may be blocked from using Section 44AD for the next five assessment years.
```

---

## Section 44AD vs Section 44ADA

Section 44AD and Section 44ADA are different.

```text
Section 44AD = Small businesses
Section 44ADA = Specified professionals
```

Examples of Section 44AD businesses:

* Retail shop
* Wholesale trader
* Small manufacturing business
* Small service business not covered under 44ADA
* Small online seller

Examples usually linked with Section 44ADA:

* Doctor
* Lawyer
* Engineer
* Architect
* Accountant
* Technical consultant
* Interior decorator

Do not confuse small business income with specified professional income.

---

## Section 44AD vs Section 44AE

Section 44AD does not apply to goods carriage businesses covered under Section 44AE.

```text
Section 44AD = General eligible small business
Section 44AE = Goods carriage business
```

Example:

```text
A small kirana store may use Section 44AD.
A small truck operator may need Section 44AE instead.
```

---

## GST and Section 44AD

GST and income tax are separate.

```text
GST return:
Reports sales, purchases, output tax, and input tax credit.
```

```text
Section 44AD:
Reports presumptive income for income tax.
```

For GST-registered businesses, turnover reported under GST should broadly match turnover considered for income tax.

Mismatch example:

```text
GST sales = ₹80,00,000
Section 44AD turnover = ₹45,00,000
```

This mismatch may create questions unless there is a valid reason.

---

## Common Mistakes Under Section 44AD

Common mistakes include:

* Using Section 44AD despite being ineligible
* Applying ₹3 crore limit even when cash receipts are more than 5%
* Treating commission income as eligible business income
* Using Section 44AD for goods carriage business instead of checking Section 44AE
* Claiming business expenses again after presumptive income
* Forgetting to include bank interest or other income separately
* Not matching turnover with GST records
* Ignoring advance tax
* Declaring income below 6% or 8% without checking audit requirements
* Opting out without understanding the five-year restriction
