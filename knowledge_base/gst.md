# GST Basics for MSMEs

## 1. Meaning of GST

GST means Goods and Services Tax.

It is an indirect tax charged on the supply of goods or services or both.

GST is called an indirect tax because the final tax burden is usually borne by the final consumer, but the tax is collected and deposited by businesses at different stages of the supply chain.

For example:

A wholesaler sells goods to a retailer.

The wholesaler charges GST on the sale.

The retailer sells the same goods to a customer and charges GST again.

The retailer can claim credit for GST already paid on purchase.

This system avoids repeated tax burden on the same value and taxes only the value addition at each stage.

## 2. Simple GST Example

Suppose a trader buys goods for ₹10,000 plus 18% GST.

Purchase value = ₹10,000
GST paid on purchase = ₹1,800
Total paid to supplier = ₹11,800

Later, the trader sells the goods for ₹15,000 plus 18% GST.

Sale value = ₹15,000
GST collected on sale = ₹2,700
Total collected from customer = ₹17,700

The trader does not pay the full ₹2,700 to the government.

The trader can adjust GST already paid on purchase.

Output GST = ₹2,700
Input GST = ₹1,800
Net GST payable = ₹900

So the trader pays only ₹900 to the government.

This is the basic idea of input tax credit.

## 3. Why GST Was Introduced

GST was introduced to create a more unified indirect tax system in India.

Before GST, businesses had to deal with many indirect taxes such as VAT, service tax, excise duty, entry tax, entertainment tax, luxury tax, and other state-level taxes.

This made compliance complex.

GST reduced many separate indirect taxes into one broader tax system.

For MSMEs, GST is important because it affects invoices, sales, purchases, pricing, accounting, cash flow, tax returns, and input tax credit.

## 4. GST Is a Destination-Based Tax

GST is generally a destination-based tax.

This means tax revenue usually goes to the state where goods or services are consumed, not necessarily where they are produced.

For example:

A supplier in Delhi sells goods to a buyer in Maharashtra.

The goods are consumed in Maharashtra.

GST treatment will depend on whether the supply is intra-state or inter-state.

For inter-state supply, IGST applies.

## 5. Types of GST

GST in India commonly appears in three main forms:

CGST
SGST
IGST

There is also UTGST for Union Territories.

## 6. CGST Meaning

CGST means Central Goods and Services Tax.

CGST is collected by the Central Government.

CGST applies on intra-state supplies.

An intra-state supply means the supplier and buyer are in the same state or same applicable Union Territory.

Example:

Seller location: Delhi
Buyer location: Delhi

This is an intra-state supply.

If GST rate is 18%, the invoice may show:

CGST = 9%
SGST = 9%

Total GST = 18%

## 7. SGST Meaning

SGST means State Goods and Services Tax.

SGST is collected by the State Government.

SGST also applies on intra-state supplies.

CGST and SGST are charged together on intra-state transactions.

Example:

Product value = ₹10,000
GST rate = 18%

CGST @ 9% = ₹900
SGST @ 9% = ₹900
Total GST = ₹1,800
Invoice total = ₹11,800

## 8. IGST Meaning

IGST means Integrated Goods and Services Tax.

IGST applies on inter-state supplies.

An inter-state supply means the supplier and buyer are in different states or applicable territories.

Example:

Seller location: Gujarat
Buyer location: Rajasthan

This is an inter-state supply.

If GST rate is 18%, the invoice may show:

IGST = 18%

Example:

Product value = ₹10,000
IGST @ 18% = ₹1,800
Invoice total = ₹11,800

## 9. CGST vs SGST vs IGST

CGST and SGST are used when sale happens within the same state.

IGST is used when sale happens between different states.

Simple rule:

Same state = CGST + SGST
Different states = IGST

Example 1:

Seller: Mumbai, Maharashtra
Buyer: Pune, Maharashtra

GST type: CGST + SGST

Example 2:

Seller: Mumbai, Maharashtra
Buyer: Jaipur, Rajasthan

GST type: IGST

## 10. UTGST Meaning

UTGST means Union Territory Goods and Services Tax.

It applies in certain Union Territories instead of SGST.

For many basic MSME cases, users mainly deal with CGST, SGST, and IGST.

## 11. GSTIN Meaning

GSTIN means Goods and Services Tax Identification Number.

It is the unique GST registration number given to a registered taxpayer.

GSTIN is used in GST invoices, GST returns, e-way bills, e-invoices, vendor verification, and input tax credit matching.

A GSTIN is usually a 15-character alphanumeric number.

It is linked to the PAN of the registered business or person.

Example GSTIN format:

22AAAAA0000A1Z5

A GSTIN helps identify:

State code
PAN of the taxpayer
Entity number
Default character
Check digit

## 12. Why GSTIN Is Important for MSMEs

GSTIN is important because it proves that a business is registered under GST.

A GST-registered seller can legally charge GST on taxable supplies.

A GST-registered buyer may need the seller’s correct GSTIN to claim input tax credit.

Wrong GSTIN on invoice can create problems in GST returns and ITC matching.

MSMEs should verify vendor GSTIN before entering purchase invoices into accounting records.

## 13. Registered Person Under GST

A registered person means a person or business registered under GST.

This can include:

Proprietorship
Partnership firm
LLP
Private limited company
Public limited company
Trust
Society
Individual service provider
Trader
Manufacturer
E-commerce seller

Once registered, the business receives a GSTIN.

## 14. Taxable Person

A taxable person is a person who is registered or required to be registered under GST.

A taxable person may be liable to collect GST, file returns, maintain records, and pay GST.

Not every small business is automatically required to register.

GST registration depends on turnover, nature of supply, state, e-commerce activity, inter-state supply, and other legal conditions.

## 15. Taxable Supply

A taxable supply means a supply of goods or services on which GST is chargeable.

If GST applies to a sale, it is a taxable supply.

Example:

Selling taxable electronics
Providing taxable consulting service
Selling taxable furniture
Providing taxable repair service

Taxable supply is important because GST liability arises on taxable supplies.

## 16. Exempt Supply

Exempt supply means supply on which GST is not charged due to exemption.

If a supply is exempt, GST is not collected from the customer.

Input tax credit related to exempt supplies is generally not available or may need reversal.

Example:

Some goods or services may be exempt under GST notifications.

MSMEs should check the latest official GST rate and exemption notifications before deciding whether GST applies.

## 17. Nil-Rated Supply

Nil-rated supply means the GST rate is 0%.

No GST is charged on the outward supply.

However, nil-rated supply is not always the same as zero-rated supply.

This distinction matters for input tax credit.

## 18. Zero-Rated Supply

Zero-rated supply generally refers to exports and supplies to SEZ under GST.

Zero-rated supply can allow certain refund or ITC benefits, subject to conditions.

This topic should be handled separately in advanced GST files because it involves export documentation, LUT, refund claims, and compliance rules.

## 19. Non-GST Supply

Some items may be outside GST.

For example, alcoholic liquor for human consumption is outside GST.

Certain petroleum products are also treated separately until notified.

Non-GST supply should not be confused with exempt supply.

## 20. Outward Supply

Outward supply means sales made by the business.

For a trader, outward supply usually means goods sold to customers.

For a service provider, outward supply means services provided to clients.

Examples:

Sale invoice issued to customer
Service invoice raised on client
Debit note issued to customer
Credit note issued to customer

Outward supply details are reported in GST returns such as GSTR-1.

## 21. Inward Supply

Inward supply means purchases or expenses received by the business.

Examples:

Goods purchased from supplier
Raw material purchased
Rent service received
Consulting service received
Courier service received
Software subscription received
Repair service received

Inward supply matters because GST paid on inward supplies may become input tax credit if eligible.

## 22. Output Tax

Output tax means GST charged by a business on its sales.

Example:

A business sells goods worth ₹50,000 plus 18% GST.

GST charged on sale = ₹9,000

This ₹9,000 is output tax.

The business collects it from the customer and later pays it to the government after adjusting eligible input tax credit.

## 23. Input Tax

Input tax means GST paid by a business on purchases and expenses.

Example:

A business buys goods worth ₹20,000 plus 18% GST.

GST paid on purchase = ₹3,600

This ₹3,600 is input tax.

If eligible, it can be claimed as input tax credit.

## 24. Input Tax Credit Meaning

Input Tax Credit is commonly called ITC.

ITC means the credit of GST paid on purchases and eligible business expenses.

A registered business can use eligible ITC to reduce GST payable on sales.

Simple formula:

Net GST payable = Output GST - Eligible Input Tax Credit

Example:

Output GST on sales = ₹25,000
Eligible ITC on purchases = ₹18,000
Net GST payable = ₹7,000

## 25. Why ITC Is Important for MSMEs

ITC directly affects cash flow.

If ITC is available, the business pays less GST in cash.

If ITC is blocked, missing, mismatched, or not recorded, the business may pay more cash to the government.

For MSMEs, ITC mistakes can create cash shortages.

Common ITC issues include:

Supplier did not file invoice properly
Wrong GSTIN on invoice
Invoice missing required fields
Goods or services not used for business
GST paid on blocked credit category
Payment not made to supplier within required time
Invoice not reflected correctly in GST portal data

## 26. Basic ITC Conditions

A business should generally claim ITC only when the purchase is related to business and proper documents are available.

Common documents for ITC include:

Tax invoice from supplier
Debit note from supplier
Bill of entry for imports
Relevant GST document issued under law

The buyer should also ensure that supplier details, GSTIN, invoice number, taxable value, GST amount, and tax type are correct.

## 27. ITC Example for Trader

A trader buys goods from a supplier.

Purchase value = ₹1,00,000
GST paid on purchase = ₹18,000
Total purchase invoice = ₹1,18,000

The trader sells the goods.

Sales value = ₹1,40,000
GST collected on sale = ₹25,200
Total sales invoice = ₹1,65,200

Output GST = ₹25,200
Input GST = ₹18,000
Net GST payable = ₹7,200

The trader does not treat ₹25,200 as profit.

GST collected from customer belongs to the government after adjusting eligible ITC.

## 28. ITC Example for Service Business

A consultant provides service worth ₹80,000 plus 18% GST.

Output GST = ₹14,400

The consultant pays GST on business expenses:

Software subscription GST = ₹2,000
Internet bill GST = ₹500
Office rent GST = ₹3,000

Eligible ITC = ₹5,500

Net GST payable = ₹14,400 - ₹5,500
Net GST payable = ₹8,900

## 29. Blocked ITC

Blocked ITC means GST credit that cannot be claimed even if GST was paid.

Some purchases may not be eligible for ITC due to legal restrictions.

Examples may include certain motor vehicles, personal expenses, food and beverages in many cases, health services in many cases, club memberships, and goods lost or destroyed.

The exact blocked ITC rules should be checked carefully under GST law.

For RAG answers, do not say every GST-paid purchase is eligible for ITC.

A safer answer is:

"GST paid on purchases may be available as ITC only if the purchase is business-related, supported by proper documents, reflected correctly in GST records, and not blocked under GST law."

## 30. GST Invoice

A GST invoice is a tax invoice issued by a registered supplier for taxable supplies.

It records:

Seller details
Buyer details
GSTIN
Invoice number
Invoice date
Description of goods or services
HSN or SAC code
Quantity
Taxable value
GST rate
CGST amount
SGST amount
IGST amount
Total invoice value
Place of supply
Signature or digital authentication where applicable

GST invoices are important for accounting, tax payment, ITC claims, audits, and business records.

## 31. B2B Invoice

B2B means Business to Business.

A B2B invoice is issued when a registered business sells to another registered business.

Example:

A registered wholesaler sells goods to a registered retailer.

B2B invoices are important because the buyer may claim ITC based on the invoice.

Correct GSTIN and invoice details are very important in B2B transactions.

## 32. B2C Invoice

B2C means Business to Consumer.

A B2C invoice is issued when a business sells to an end consumer.

Example:

A retail shop sells a product to an individual customer.

The customer may not have GST registration.

B2C invoice reporting rules may differ from B2B invoice reporting rules.

## 33. HSN Code

HSN means Harmonized System of Nomenclature.

It is used for classification of goods.

HSN helps identify the type of product and applicable GST rate.

Example:

Different products may have different HSN codes and GST rates.

MSMEs should avoid guessing HSN codes because wrong classification can lead to wrong GST rate, wrong invoice, and tax disputes.

## 34. SAC Code

SAC means Services Accounting Code.

It is used for classification of services.

Example:

Consulting services, repair services, transport services, and professional services may have different SAC codes.

SAC helps in GST rate identification and return reporting.

## 35. Place of Supply

Place of supply is used to determine whether a transaction is intra-state or inter-state.

It helps decide whether to charge CGST + SGST or IGST.

For goods, place of supply often depends on movement of goods and delivery location.

For services, rules can vary depending on type of service.

MSMEs should be careful with place of supply because wrong treatment can lead to incorrect GST type.

## 36. Intra-State Supply

Intra-state supply means supplier location and place of supply are in the same state.

GST charged:

CGST + SGST

Example:

Supplier: Karnataka
Place of supply: Karnataka

GST type: CGST + SGST

## 37. Inter-State Supply

Inter-state supply means supplier location and place of supply are in different states.

GST charged:

IGST

Example:

Supplier: Karnataka
Place of supply: Tamil Nadu

GST type: IGST

## 38. Reverse Charge Mechanism

Reverse Charge Mechanism is commonly called RCM.

Normally, the supplier collects GST from the buyer and pays it to the government.

Under reverse charge, the recipient of goods or services pays GST directly to the government.

RCM applies only in specified cases.

Example:

Certain notified services may require the recipient to pay GST under reverse charge.

RCM can affect cash flow because GST may need to be paid in cash first, even if ITC is later available.

## 39. Composition Scheme

The composition scheme is a simplified GST scheme for eligible small taxpayers.

It generally allows lower compliance burden and payment of tax at a prescribed composition rate.

However, composition taxpayers usually cannot collect GST separately from customers and cannot pass ITC to buyers.

A composition dealer usually cannot issue a normal tax invoice for GST collection.

This scheme may be useful for small local businesses, but it may not be suitable for businesses selling mainly to GST-registered buyers who want ITC.

## 40. Regular GST Scheme

Under the regular GST scheme, the taxpayer charges GST on outward taxable supplies, claims eligible ITC, files regular GST returns, and pays net GST liability.

Regular GST is common for businesses that:

Sell to GST-registered buyers
Need to pass ITC to customers
Have inter-state business
Want to claim ITC on purchases
Deal with B2B invoices

## 41. GST Returns

GST returns are periodic filings submitted by registered taxpayers.

Returns contain details of sales, purchases, tax collected, input tax credit, tax payable, and tax paid.

For MSMEs, GST returns are important because they connect invoices, accounting records, ITC, tax payment, and compliance status.

Common GST returns include:

GSTR-1
GSTR-3B
GSTR-9
CMP-08 for composition taxpayers

Not every taxpayer files every return.

Return applicability depends on registration type, turnover, scheme, and notifications.

## 42. GSTR-1 Meaning

GSTR-1 is a return or statement for outward supplies.

It contains details of sales invoices, debit notes, credit notes, exports, and other outward supply information.

For a normal taxpayer, GSTR-1 helps report invoice-level sales data.

B2B invoice details reported in GSTR-1 can affect the buyer’s ITC visibility.

If a seller does not report an invoice correctly, the buyer may face ITC issues.

## 43. GSTR-3B Meaning

GSTR-3B is a summary return.

It is used to declare summary GST liability for a tax period and pay tax.

GSTR-3B usually includes:

Outward taxable supplies
Tax payable
Eligible ITC
ITC reversed
Tax paid through ITC
Tax paid through cash
Interest and late fee if applicable

GSTR-3B is not just a formality.

It directly affects GST payment.

## 44. Difference Between GSTR-1 and GSTR-3B

GSTR-1 reports outward supply details.

GSTR-3B reports summary tax liability and tax payment.

Simple difference:

GSTR-1 = detailed sales reporting
GSTR-3B = summary tax payment return

Example:

A business makes 100 sales invoices in a month.

GSTR-1 reports invoice-level details.

GSTR-3B reports total taxable sales, total GST liability, eligible ITC, and net tax payable.

## 45. Nil Return

A nil return is filed when there is no business activity in the tax period, but the taxpayer is still required to file the return.

For example:

No sales
No purchases
No GST liability
No ITC claim

Even in such cases, registered taxpayers may still need to file nil returns depending on return requirement.

Not filing nil returns can lead to late fees and compliance problems.

## 46. GST Payment

GST payment is made through the GST portal.

Tax can be paid using:

Input tax credit balance
Cash ledger balance

Some liabilities, such as interest, penalty, or late fee, may need to be paid in cash.

MSMEs should track GST payable before due dates to avoid cash flow pressure.

## 47. Electronic Credit Ledger

The electronic credit ledger contains eligible input tax credit.

When a business claims ITC, it appears in the credit ledger.

This credit can be used to pay eligible GST liabilities according to GST utilization rules.

## 48. Electronic Cash Ledger

The electronic cash ledger contains cash deposited by the taxpayer.

It is used to pay tax, interest, penalty, fee, or other amounts.

If ITC is not enough, the business pays remaining GST through cash ledger.

## 49. Electronic Liability Register

The electronic liability register shows GST liabilities.

It records tax payable, interest, late fee, penalty, and other dues.

A business should reconcile the liability register with books and returns.

## 50. GST Rate

GST rate means the percentage of GST charged on a product or service.

Common GST rates include rates such as 0%, 5%, 12%, 18%, and 28%, but rates can change through notifications and GST Council recommendations.

MSMEs should always verify the latest GST rate from official sources before invoicing.

Wrong GST rate can lead to underpayment, overpayment, customer disputes, and return mismatch.

## 51. GST Calculation Formula

Basic GST formula:

GST amount = Taxable value × GST rate

Invoice total = Taxable value + GST amount

Example:

Taxable value = ₹20,000
GST rate = 18%

GST amount = ₹20,000 × 18%
GST amount = ₹3,600

Invoice total = ₹23,600

## 52. GST-Inclusive Price

GST-inclusive price means the displayed price already includes GST.

Example:

Final selling price = ₹11,800
GST rate = 18%

Taxable value = ₹11,800 × 100 / 118
Taxable value = ₹10,000

GST amount = ₹1,800

This is useful for retail businesses that quote final prices to customers.

## 53. GST-Exclusive Price

GST-exclusive price means GST is added separately over the base price.

Example:

Base price = ₹10,000
GST rate = 18%
GST amount = ₹1,800
Invoice total = ₹11,800

This is common in B2B quotations and invoices.

## 54. Credit Note

A credit note is issued when the taxable value or GST charged in an earlier invoice needs to be reduced.

Common reasons:

Sales return
Post-sale discount
Overbilling
Wrong GST charged
Invoice value reduced

Example:

Original invoice value = ₹50,000
Goods returned = ₹10,000

Supplier may issue a credit note for ₹10,000 plus related GST adjustment.

## 55. Debit Note

A debit note is issued when the taxable value or GST charged in an earlier invoice needs to be increased.

Common reasons:

Underbilling
Extra quantity supplied
Price increase
GST amount short charged

Example:

Original invoice value = ₹50,000
Correct value should be ₹55,000

Supplier may issue a debit note for ₹5,000 plus GST.

## 56. GST and MSME Cash Flow

GST affects MSME cash flow strongly.

A business may collect GST from customers but must pay it to the government.

If customers delay payment, the business may still face GST payment pressure.

If suppliers delay return filing, the buyer may face ITC problems.

If invoices are incorrect, ITC may get blocked or delayed.

If accounting records are not updated, GST payable may be miscalculated.

Good GST tracking helps MSMEs avoid sudden tax payments.

## 57. GST and Working Capital

GST can affect working capital in several ways:

GST paid on purchases may be locked until ITC is claimed.

GST on sales may become payable before customer payment is received.

Refund claims may take time.

Wrong invoicing may delay ITC.

RCM may require upfront cash payment.

MSMEs should include GST timing in cash flow planning.

## 58. GST and Accounting

GST should be recorded separately from sales and purchases.

Example sales invoice:

Sales value = ₹1,00,000
Output GST = ₹18,000
Customer receivable = ₹1,18,000

Accounting should not treat the full ₹1,18,000 as revenue.

Revenue is ₹1,00,000.

GST collected is a liability until paid or adjusted.

Example purchase invoice:

Purchase value = ₹50,000
Input GST = ₹9,000
Supplier payable = ₹59,000

If ITC is eligible, ₹9,000 is recorded as input GST credit, not as expense.

## 60. GST Reconciliation

GST reconciliation means comparing GST data from different sources.

A business may compare:

Sales register with GSTR-1
Sales register with GSTR-3B
Purchase register with ITC data
Books with GST portal
GST payable as per books with GST paid
Credit notes in books with GST returns
Debit notes in books with GST returns

Reconciliation helps identify mismatch before filing or before audit.

## 64. Simple GST Flow for MSME

Step 1: Buy goods or services from supplier.
Step 2: Record purchase invoice and input GST.
Step 3: Sell goods or services to customer.
Step 4: Issue GST invoice and charge output GST.
Step 5: Report outward supplies in GSTR-1.
Step 6: Calculate GST payable in GSTR-3B.
Step 7: Adjust eligible ITC.
Step 8: Pay remaining GST through cash ledger.
Step 9: File return.
Step 10: Reconcile books with GST portal.

## 65. Example Full GST Cycle

A small electronics trader buys products worth ₹2,00,000 plus 18% GST.

Purchase value = ₹2,00,000
Input GST = ₹36,000
Supplier payable = ₹2,36,000

The trader sells products worth ₹3,00,000 plus 18% GST.

Sales value = ₹3,00,000
Output GST = ₹54,000
Customer receivable = ₹3,54,000

GST calculation:

Output GST = ₹54,000
Eligible ITC = ₹36,000
Net GST payable = ₹18,000

Accounting view:

Sales revenue = ₹3,00,000
GST collected = ₹54,000 liability
Purchase cost = ₹2,00,000
Input GST = ₹36,000 credit
Net GST cash payment = ₹18,000

Business profit calculation should not include GST collected as income.

## 67. Common Questions and Answers

### What is GST?

GST is Goods and Services Tax. It is an indirect tax on supply of goods or services or both.

### What is CGST?

CGST is Central GST. It is charged with SGST on intra-state supplies.

### What is SGST?

SGST is State GST. It is charged with CGST on intra-state supplies.

### What is IGST?

IGST is Integrated GST. It is charged on inter-state supplies.

### When should CGST and SGST be charged?

CGST and SGST should generally be charged when supplier location and place of supply are in the same state.

### When should IGST be charged?

IGST should generally be charged when supplier location and place of supply are in different states.

### What is GSTIN?

GSTIN is the GST registration number of a registered taxpayer.

### What is ITC?

ITC means Input Tax Credit. It is the credit of eligible GST paid on purchases and business expenses.

### What is GSTR-1?

GSTR-1 is used to report outward supply details such as sales invoices, credit notes, and debit notes.

### What is GSTR-3B?

GSTR-3B is a summary return used to declare GST liability, claim eligible ITC, and pay tax.
