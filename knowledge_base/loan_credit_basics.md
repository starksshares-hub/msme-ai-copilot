# Loan and Credit Basics for Small Businesses

## Purpose of this document

This document explains the basic concepts of business loans and credit for small businesses, MSMEs, shop owners, traders, manufacturers, and service providers.

It is useful for answering questions about business loans, EMI, interest, collateral, credit score, cash credit, working capital finance, repayment risk, and loan eligibility.

---

## What is a business loan?

A business loan is money borrowed by a business from a bank, NBFC, government-backed lender, or financial institution.

The business agrees to repay the borrowed amount along with interest over a fixed period or according to agreed credit terms.

Business loans are usually taken for:

* Buying machinery or equipment
* Purchasing stock or raw materials
* Expanding a shop, office, or factory
* Managing cash flow problems
* Paying suppliers before customer payments arrive
* Hiring employees
* Repairing or upgrading business assets
* Starting a new business unit
* Funding seasonal demand

A loan is not income. It increases cash immediately, but it also creates a liability because the money must be repaid.

---

## Important loan terms

### Principal

Principal is the original amount borrowed.

Example:

If a business borrows ₹5,00,000, the principal is ₹5,00,000.

---

### Interest

Interest is the cost of borrowing money.

It is the extra amount paid to the lender in addition to the principal.

Example:

If a business borrows ₹5,00,000 and repays ₹5,60,000 in total, then ₹60,000 is the interest cost.

---

### Interest rate

The interest rate is usually shown as a percentage per year.

Example:

If the annual interest rate is 12%, the borrower pays interest based on that rate.

Interest rates may depend on:

* Loan type
* Borrower’s credit score
* Business turnover
* Profitability
* Collateral offered
* Repayment history
* Bank policy
* Market interest rates
* Loan tenure
* Risk level of the business

---

### EMI

EMI means Equated Monthly Instalment.

It is the fixed monthly amount paid by the borrower to repay a loan.

An EMI usually contains two parts:

1. Principal repayment
2. Interest payment

In the early months of many loans, the interest portion may be higher. Over time, the principal portion increases.

Basic EMI formula:

```text
EMI = [P × R × (1 + R)^N] / [(1 + R)^N - 1]
```

Where:

* P = loan principal
* R = monthly interest rate
* N = number of monthly instalments

Example:

A business takes a term loan and repays it every month through EMI. The EMI affects monthly cash flow and should be checked before taking the loan.

---

## Term loan

A term loan is a loan given for a fixed amount and repaid over a fixed period.

Common uses:

* Buying machinery
* Buying commercial vehicles
* Renovating a shop
* Expanding factory capacity
* Setting up a new branch
* Long-term business investment

Features of a term loan:

* Fixed loan amount
* Fixed repayment schedule
* EMI-based repayment
* Specific loan tenure
* May require collateral
* Used mostly for long-term assets or expansion

Example:

A bakery takes a ₹10,00,000 term loan to buy an oven and repay it over 5 years.

---

## Working capital loan

A working capital loan is used to manage day-to-day business expenses.

It is not usually used for buying long-term assets.

Common uses:

* Buying inventory
* Paying suppliers
* Paying wages
* Managing rent and electricity bills
* Covering delay in customer payments
* Handling seasonal sales demand

Example:

A garment trader needs money to buy stock before the festive season. The trader may take a working capital loan and repay it after sales happen.

---

## Cash credit

Cash credit is a short-term working capital facility given to businesses.

Under cash credit, the bank sanctions a credit limit. The business can withdraw money up to that limit as needed.

Interest is usually charged on the amount actually used, not always on the full sanctioned limit.

Cash credit is often linked to business stock, receivables, or current assets.

Example:

A wholesaler has a cash credit limit of ₹8,00,000.

If the wholesaler uses only ₹3,00,000, interest is charged mainly on ₹3,00,000, not the full ₹8,00,000.

Cash credit is useful when business cash needs change frequently.

---

## Overdraft

An overdraft allows the account holder to withdraw more money than the available bank balance, up to an approved limit.

It is similar to cash credit, but overdrafts may be given against deposits, property, or business relationship strength.

Example:

If a business account has ₹20,000 but has an overdraft limit of ₹2,00,000, the business may be able to withdraw more than ₹20,000 up to the allowed limit.

---

## Difference between term loan and cash credit

| Point        | Term Loan                      | Cash Credit                     |
| ------------ | ------------------------------ | ------------------------------- |
| Purpose      | Long-term asset or expansion   | Working capital                 |
| Disbursement | Full amount usually given once | Withdraw as needed              |
| Repayment    | EMI or fixed schedule          | Flexible usage and repayment    |
| Interest     | On outstanding loan amount     | Usually on utilized amount      |
| Best for     | Machinery, vehicle, expansion  | Inventory, suppliers, cash flow |
| Tenure       | Medium to long term            | Short term, often renewed       |

## Collateral

Collateral is an asset given as security for a loan.

If the borrower fails to repay, the lender may have the right to recover dues from the collateral as per loan agreement and law.

Common collateral examples:

* Land
* Building
* Machinery
* Fixed deposit
* Gold
* Inventory
* Receivables
* Property documents
* Vehicles

Collateral reduces the lender’s risk.

A secured loan has collateral.

An unsecured loan does not have collateral, but it may have a higher interest rate or stricter eligibility.

---

## Hypothecation

Hypothecation means the borrower offers movable assets as security, but keeps using them in the business.

Common hypothecated assets:

* Stock
* Raw materials
* Finished goods
* Vehicles
* Machinery
* Receivables

Example:

A trader takes cash credit against stock. The stock remains with the trader, but the bank has a security interest over it.

---

## Credit score

A credit score shows how reliable a borrower has been in repaying past loans and credit dues.

For individuals, credit score is based on personal borrowing history.

For businesses, lenders may check:

* Business credit report
* Owner’s credit score
* Company credit history
* Existing loans
* Repayment record
* Cheque bounce history
* Defaults
* Credit utilization
* Tax and financial records
* Bank statements

A good credit profile can improve the chances of loan approval.

A weak credit profile may lead to rejection, lower loan amount, higher interest rate, or demand for collateral.

---

## CIBIL score and CIBIL rank

In India, lenders often use credit bureau reports such as CIBIL reports while evaluating borrowers.

For individuals, a higher credit score generally shows better repayment history.

For companies, CIBIL Rank may be used. CIBIL Rank is given on a scale from 10 to 1, where 1 is the best rank.

Important note:

Credit score alone does not guarantee loan approval. Lenders also check business income, bank statements, profitability, collateral, existing debt, repayment capacity, and documentation.

---

## Repayment capacity

Repayment capacity means whether the business can comfortably repay the loan.

Lenders may check:

* Monthly sales
* Monthly profit
* Cash flow
* Existing EMIs
* Supplier dues
* Customer receivables
* Bank balance
* Business stability
* Seasonality
* Owner’s financial discipline

A business should not take a loan only because it is available. It should check whether future cash flow can handle repayment.

---

## Debt burden

Debt burden means the pressure created by existing loans and repayment obligations.

High debt burden can harm a business.

Signs of high debt burden:

* Too many EMIs
* New loans taken to repay old loans
* Frequent cheque bounces
* Delayed supplier payments
* Low bank balance after EMI payments
* Using business loan for personal expenses
* No clear repayment plan
* Falling profits despite rising loans

---

## Good loan reasons

A business loan may be useful when it helps the business earn more, save costs, or survive a temporary cash gap.

Good reasons may include:

* Buying machinery that increases production
* Purchasing fast-moving inventory
* Expanding a profitable business
* Taking bulk purchase discounts
* Managing genuine working capital gaps
* Replacing expensive debt with cheaper debt
* Funding confirmed customer orders
* Improving business infrastructure

---

## Bad loan reasons

A loan can become dangerous if used without planning.

Risky reasons include:

* Taking a loan without knowing EMI
* Borrowing to cover continuous losses
* Using business loans for personal luxury spending
* Taking high-interest loans without comparing options
* Borrowing without sales records
* Taking new loans to hide poor cash flow
* Expanding before demand is proven
* Ignoring hidden charges and penalties

---

* PAN card
* Aadhaar card
* Business registration proof
* Udyam registration
* GST registration, if applicable
* Bank statements
* ITR documents
* Profit and loss statement
* Balance sheet
* Sales records
* Purchase records

Exact requirements depend on lender, loan type, loan size, and business structure.

## Loan charges

A borrower should check the full cost of the loan, not only the interest rate.

Common charges may include:

* Processing fee
* Documentation charge
* Legal verification charge
* Valuation charge
* Insurance cost
* Late payment penalty
* Prepayment charge
* Foreclosure charge
* Renewal charge
* GST on fees
* Stamp duty, if applicable

The real cost of borrowing may be higher than the stated interest rate.

---

## Fixed interest rate

A fixed interest rate remains the same for the agreed period.

Benefits:

* Predictable EMI
* Easier planning
* Less risk from rate increases

Limitations:

* May be higher than floating rate at the start
* May not reduce when market rates fall
* Prepayment rules may differ

---

## Floating interest rate

A floating interest rate can change based on benchmark rates and lender policy.

Benefits:

* May reduce if benchmark rates fall
* Sometimes starts lower than fixed rate

Limitations:

* EMI or tenure may change
* Cash flow planning becomes harder
* Borrower carries interest rate risk

---

## Secured loan

A secured loan is backed by collateral.

Example:

A business takes a loan against property, machinery, stock, or fixed deposit.

Benefits:

* Higher chance of approval
* Larger loan amount possible
* May have lower interest rate

Risks:

* Collateral may be at risk if repayment fails
* More documentation
* Longer approval process

---

## Unsecured loan

An unsecured loan does not require collateral.

Example:

A small business takes a collateral-free business loan based on turnover and bank statements.

Benefits:

* Faster processing
* No asset security required
* Useful for small urgent needs

Risks:

* Higher interest rate
* Lower loan amount
* Strict eligibility
* Personal guarantee may still be required

---

## Credit guarantee

Credit guarantee means a guarantee institution covers part of the lender’s risk.

In India, CGTMSE supports collateral-free or partially collateral-backed credit facilities for eligible micro and small enterprises through member lending institutions.

This does not mean every borrower automatically gets a loan.

The lender still checks eligibility, viability, repayment capacity, documents, and scheme rules.

## Loan approval factors

A lender may consider:

* Business vintage
* Sales turnover
* Profit margin
* Bank transactions
* GST returns
* Income tax returns
* Existing debt
* Repayment history
* Credit score
* Collateral
* Industry risk
* Purpose of loan
* Business owner experience
* Cash flow strength
* Customer concentration
* Supplier stability

---

## EMI and cash flow

EMI should be compared with monthly cash flow.

A business should ask:

* Can I pay EMI even in a weak sales month?
* Will EMI affect supplier payments?
* Will EMI reduce working capital too much?
* What happens if customers delay payment?
* Do I have emergency cash reserve?
* Is the loan helping generate enough extra income?

A loan that looks affordable on paper may become risky if the business has irregular cash flow.

---

## Example: loan decision

A furniture business wants to buy a machine worth ₹6,00,000.

Before taking the loan, the owner should check:

* Expected increase in production
* Expected increase in sales
* Monthly EMI
* Maintenance cost
* Electricity cost
* Worker training cost
* Demand for extra output
* Payback period
* Profit after EMI

If the new machine increases monthly profit by ₹40,000 and EMI is ₹18,000, the loan may be useful.

If the machine increases profit by only ₹10,000 and EMI is ₹18,000, the loan may create cash pressure.

---

## Red flags before taking a loan

Warning signs:

* No written loan agreement
* Unknown lender
* Very high processing fee
* Pressure to sign quickly
* No clarity on interest rate
* No repayment schedule
* Hidden penalty clauses
* Blank documents requested
* Personal documents taken without receipt
* EMI more than business can handle
* Loan used for non-business spending
* No comparison with other lenders

---

## Red flags after taking a loan

Warning signs:

* EMI missed regularly
* Supplier payments delayed due to EMI
* New borrowing needed every month
* Sales not increasing after loan
* Bank account frequently overdrawn
* Interest cost rising faster than profit
* Business owner avoids checking statements
* Cash credit used permanently like a long-term loan
* Stock financed by loan is not selling

---

## How to improve loan eligibility

A business can improve loan eligibility by:

* Maintaining proper invoices
* Filing tax returns on time
* Keeping GST records accurate
* Avoiding cheque bounces
* Paying EMIs on time
* Reducing unnecessary debt
* Maintaining bank balance discipline
* Preparing profit and loss statements
* Preparing balance sheets
* Keeping stock records
* Separating personal and business expenses
* Building a good credit history
* Keeping customer payment records
* Avoiding fake invoices
* Maintaining Udyam registration, if applicable

---

## Simple loan checklist

Before taking a business loan, check:

* Why do I need the loan?
* How much do I actually need?
* What is the interest rate?
* What is the EMI?
* What is the tenure?
* What are the extra charges?
* Is collateral required?
* Can I repay during slow months?
* What happens if I miss EMI?
* Is there a prepayment penalty?
* Is the lender trusted?
* Will the loan increase profit or only increase pressure?

## Common user questions this document can answer

### What is a business loan?

A business loan is borrowed money used for business purposes. It must be repaid with interest.

### What is EMI?

EMI is the fixed monthly payment made to repay a loan. It usually includes both principal and interest.

### What is collateral?

Collateral is an asset given as security for a loan.

### What is cash credit?

Cash credit is a working capital facility where a business can withdraw money up to a sanctioned limit and usually pays interest on the utilized amount.

### What is the difference between a term loan and cash credit?

A term loan is usually for long-term needs and has fixed repayment. Cash credit is usually for working capital and flexible withdrawals.

### Does good credit score guarantee loan approval?

No. A good credit score helps, but lenders also check income, cash flow, documents, collateral, business stability, and repayment capacity.

### What is a secured loan?

A secured loan is backed by collateral.

### What is an unsecured loan?

An unsecured loan does not require collateral but may have higher interest rates and stricter eligibility.

### Why is EMI important for small businesses?

EMI affects monthly cash flow. If EMI is too high, the business may struggle to pay suppliers, rent, salaries, and other expenses.

### What documents are needed for a business loan?

Common documents include PAN, Aadhaar, bank statements, ITR, GST records, business registration, financial statements, and loan purpose documents.
